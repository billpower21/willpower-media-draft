Wellness House — Panel 3

Six months in and already in court

Sam Nebel co-founded Good Wipes and was six months into the business when a competitor hit them with an IP lawsuit. Not a cease-and-desist. An actual lawsuit.

12.5 years later, Good Wipes is in stores across the country and Sam is on a panel at Willpower talking about what he learned. The lawsuit didn't kill the company. But it introduced him early to something every long-term founder knows: there is always a version of this waiting for you. The question is whether you're going to be the person still standing when it passes.

"Every year is a new experience." That's Sam's thesis on what 12-plus years actually looks like. The IP lawsuit was year one. Eleven more years of new experiences since.

The Sri Lanka call

Hila Stoch built 437 Activewear and came within one phone call of not having it anymore.

Her entire production was based in Sri Lanka. The country's economy collapsed in 2022. She picked up the phone and called her factory contact, not knowing if the factory was still operating, if the people she worked with still had jobs, or if the inventory she needed even existed. The call went through. The factory was running. She shipped.

She described it as one of the most clarifying moments of her career. Every founder talks about resilience as an abstract quality. Hila's version: your whole business is sometimes contingent on a phone call going a certain way. You either make the call or you don't.

She made it.

"Taking back creative control after years of playing it safe was the turning point. We had to be willing to lose some customers to gain the right ones."

Hila Stoch, Founder, 437 Activewear

The four-day work week at a company with no investors

437 has run a four-day work week for three straight years. No investor pressure to reverse it. No VC asking about productivity data. No board questioning the model.

Hila built the company without outside capital, which means she runs it however she wants. The four-day week was a decision about what kind of company she wanted to lead. The result: retention, morale, and a team that actually wants to be there on the four days they show up.

The counterintuitive read for founders: the companies with the most flexibility to build the way they want are often the ones who haven't taken outside money. The trade-off is slower capital access. The gain is that you actually decide how your company operates.

How Porta Palace became a marketing strategy by accident

Brian at It's That Simple has 10,000 grocery store locations and a product called Porta Palace. He did not design it as a guerrilla marketing play. It evolved into one.

The concept: a mobile setup that goes to 15 to 20 events per year and turns any space into a brand experience. It generates content. It builds relationships with people who become long-term customers and advocates. It creates the kind of word-of-mouth that no grocery store shelf placement can replicate on its own.

The scale of their retail distribution means they need both. The stores get them passive availability. Porta Palace events get them active evangelists.

"Know what your business is built for. Chasing every opportunity sounds good until you realize you've optimized for nothing."

Brian, Founder, It's That Simple

Know what your business is actually built for

Brian's sharpest moment of the panel: every business has a lane, and the ones that get in trouble are the ones that chase opportunities outside of it.

It's That Simple is built for grocery retail. Not DTC, not restaurants, not B2B ingredients. When an opportunity comes along that looks like a short-term win but pulls them out of that lane, the answer is no. "Every opportunity can look good on paper. Most of them aren't right for your business."

Sam's version from 12.5 years at Good Wipes: the clearest signal of a business drifting off track is when founders start taking meetings they know they shouldn't take. The distraction isn't the problem. The willingness to be distracted is.

Hila on what taking back creative control actually required

When 437 was younger, Hila made product and marketing decisions to please as many people as possible. Safe colors. Styles that wouldn't offend. Content that wouldn't polarize. It worked, sort of. Growth was slow. The brand felt like dozens of other activewear companies.

Taking back creative control meant making decisions that would lose some customers on purpose. Bolder designs. A more specific aesthetic. A clearer point of view on who 437 is for and who it isn't right now. The customers who left were replaced by customers who stayed much longer and became actual advocates. "You have to be willing to lose some customers to gain the right ones."

What to take from the room

  • Sam is 12.5 years in. The IP lawsuit was year one. If you're in year one of something hard, the only real data point is whether you're going to be standing in year 12. That's it.
  • Hila's creative control pivot produced better retention from the customers who stayed. A brand that means something specific is worth more to fewer people and more to the right people. That math works out over time.
  • Brian's lane discipline is a framework. Write down what your business is actually optimized for. Then ask whether each new opportunity fits inside that or pulls you out of it.
  • 437's four-day work week has run three consecutive years without reverting. The businesses with the most structural flexibility are often the ones without outside capital setting the rules.
  • The Porta Palace model scales to any brand with a physical product. Find the activation format that creates advocates, then make it a repeating event rather than a one-time launch stunt.
