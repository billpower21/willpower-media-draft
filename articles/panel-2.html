Whoop's CMO Ran a Data Experiment With the Department of Defense. Here's What Both Teams Learned.

The bomb squad experiment

Whoop partnered with the Department of Defense to put devices on bomb disposal units. The question: could wearable data improve decision-making in high-stakes environments where a bad call gets people killed?

John Sullivan said the result changed how he thinks about data entirely. Bomb squad operators could see exactly how their physiology responded to specific situations. When they reviewed it, they weren't looking at abstract trends. They were watching themselves. Their own strain, their own recovery, their own stress markers tied to specific moments in the field. That is the data use case Whoop cares about: a mirror, not a scoreboard.

"We're in the business of behavior change," John said. The bomb squad was one of the clearest examples he's seen of data actually doing that work. When the stakes are real and the feedback is specific, people change how they operate.

The campaign that worked too well

Whoop launched "Be Obsessed" to celebrate people who push their limits. It resonated with exactly the audience they were targeting. Then it attracted a crowd they weren't targeting: people who bragged about terrible sleep scores, extreme overtraining, 10-day streaks of red recovery as a flex.

The campaign built hype. It also pulled in users who fundamentally misunderstood what Whoop was for. Those users churned.

John's lesson: brand energy is easy to generate. Brand meaning is harder to protect. The community you attract with your loudest marketing is often not your healthiest long-term community. "We would rather slow down our marketing approach than attract the wrong member at the wrong time."

"We always want to attract the right member rather than the wrong member. We would rather slow down our marketing approach than attract the wrong member at the wrong time."

John Sullivan, CMO, Whoop (9 years)

The HealthSpan screenshot that broke their sharing record in a week

Whoop launched a HealthSpan metric and within one week it became the most shared screenshot in the app's history.

John's explanation: members had been using recovery and strain data to optimize today. HealthSpan showed them the cumulative picture. How many years of healthy life you're building or subtracting over time, not how recovered you are on a Tuesday.

That framing landed differently. It made the data feel consequential in a new way. People shared it because it said something meaningful about them. Then John mentioned the data point that hit him personally: he discovered his APOB cholesterol was five times the outer limit. No symptoms. No warning. A number on a screen that changed what he does. "That's why I believe in what we're building."

Higher Dose's pivot: the NYC spa discovery

Ingrid Millman-Cordy and her co-founder were running a biohacking spa in New York City. They wanted to offer infrared sauna but couldn't afford the full buildout. A vendor came to them with a portable sauna blanket. They said yes, hoping it would be a small supplementary product.

It became the entire business.

The blanket allowed them to take the sauna experience out of the facility and put it in people's homes. Suddenly accessible to people who didn't live near a Higher Dose location, couldn't afford a $50-per-session facility visit, and wanted the same benefits on their own schedule. They're now on the fourth generation (V4), and a 30% repeat purchase rate tells you the community around it is real.

"The data is a weather forecast, not a report card. Use it to plan, not to grade yourself."

John Sullivan, CMO, Whoop

Data as weather forecast

This is the framing John uses now, internally and externally. A report card is a judgment of what already happened. A weather forecast is a tool for what you're going to do next.

When Whoop members look at their recovery score, the question shouldn't be "did I do well?" It should be "what am I doing today?" That reframe changes how people relate to the data and, consequently, how long they stay. Members who use Whoop as a weather forecast stick around. The ones who use it as a report card burn out or churn.

Higher Dose applies the same logic through a different door. Their products are built for daily ritual that compounds. "You feel better, you look better, and you want to do it again," Ingrid said. The feedback loop is the product.

What Ingrid is watching on community

Ingrid's most honest moment on stage: Higher Dose's community skews heavily female because that's who found them first and who they built around. She is now actively thinking about how to grow the male community without losing what makes the brand trusted among women. "We're being very intentional about not alienating the audience that got us here while also growing."

John's version: Whoop's community is global, obsessive, and evangelical. But the evangelism has to be based on real behavior change. A member who actually modifies their training and sleep because of Whoop is worth ten who wear it as a status signal. Retention is the proof.

What to take from the room

  • John's bomb squad experiment is the data use case to study. High stakes, specific feedback, real behavior change. If your product data can do that for your customer, you have something. If it's decorative, you don't.
  • The "Be Obsessed" lesson: the community you attract with your biggest campaign may not be the one that stays. Know who you're actually building with before you pick your loudest message.
  • Ingrid's sauna blanket pivot is a product category creation story. They didn't set out to democratize infrared sauna. They solved an affordability problem in their own facility and accidentally built a category.
  • Weather forecast framing works for any data product. If your customers are using your data to grade themselves rather than to plan, they will churn. Reframe the data before it goes out the door.
  • John's APOB discovery is the story that closes every Whoop pitch. A personal data point that would have been invisible without the test. Find the equivalent story in your product and use it.
