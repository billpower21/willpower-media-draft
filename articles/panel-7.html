The Memphis in May moment

Bill Neff at Yeti described the clearest proof he's seen of what community marketing actually looks like. Leanne Bakunas, who runs Yeti's culinary community, had spent years building relationships with top pit masters. At Memphis in May (the country's largest barbecue competition), Bill walked through with her. "It was like we owned the place. She was walking around like the mayor. But we weren't marketing."

Yeti was just the trusted product inside the barbecue community, and the community was doing everything else. No activation budget. No event sponsorship. No logo placement. Just 10 years of showing up for the people who loved what they loved.

Travis Rice and the European expansion Yeti didn't plan

Travis Rice is a Yeti snowboard ambassador. He wanted to fund a competition called Natural Selection. Yeti supported it because that is what they do for ambassadors. They were not trying to break into Europe. That was not the plan.

Within weeks of the competition, shops across Europe were calling to carry Yeti. "We didn't want to go sponsor a snowboard competition to get into Europe. We wanted to support our ambassador. And then it started to ripple."

Bill's lesson from both moments: the network that grows at conversation speed is slower than the one you can buy with a digital campaign. And it is the one that doesn't evaporate when the algorithm changes.

"If you domino everything back in those word-of-mouth transactions, it can get all the way back to Flip Pallot on almost every single person here."

Bill Neff, Yeti

200 ambassadors, 14 passion categories, no follower floor

Yeti works with over 200 ambassadors across fishing, hunting, barbecue, snowboarding, and more than a dozen other categories where a Yeti product functions as a tool in someone's specific pursuit. The philosophy: find people who are relevant for your product within that pursuit, let the human network take over, and don't try to accelerate it with a platform that gets eyeballs quickly but builds nothing lasting.

"Whenever I get someone who loves Yeti, I ask how they learned about it. Generally speaking, it's from a friend."

Slower. That is the point.

Tecovas: Western identity walked in and never left

Krista Dalton joined Tecovas as CMO and CDO after the brand had already built 50-plus retail locations on a D2C-first model. What she heard from everyone she talked to when evaluating the company: "I've been to your store, they gave me a margarita, and I loved it." Not the boots. The store experience.

Western as a category can be intimidating. Tecovas stores solve that by welcoming anyone without judgment. A customer walked in before a Beyonce Cowboy Carter concert and said, "Can you just dress me?" The associate did. That is community built into the retail design, not the marketing deck.

Tecovas has a Facebook group called Tecovas Enthusiasts that the brand did not start. Customers started it. When Krista's customer insights team reached out to interview members, they offered no payment or product. Just asked for feedback. Members gave hour-long sessions and came out as evangelists. "We didn't give them anything for these interviews, but they were so thankful." Listening without an agenda turned the most loyal customers into the loudest advocates.

"Don't have an agenda. Just be with the community. We don't worry about culture because the community shapes culture. If you're in the community, you're going to be in the culture."

Bill Neff, Yeti

Estee Lauder: protecting 15 brands without letting them bleed into each other

Kumkum Pinto manages product across Estee Lauder's portfolio: MAC, Jo Malone, La Mer, Clinique, Aveda, The Ordinary. Each brand has its own distinct community and identity. The company's rule: never touch the identity. What gets shared across brands is the infrastructure behind the scenes, playbooks, data frameworks, measurement criteria. The communities and identities stay completely separate.

The signal Kumkum watches for community breakdown: engagement and loyalty metrics starting to erode. Every week, Estee Lauder reviews voice-of-customer metrics and loyalty performance across D2C and retail. Most of their consumer care agents are former in-store sales associates, people who sold the products face-to-face and understand the brand from the customer's perspective, not the corporate one.

The most interesting force in Estee Lauder's innovation cycle right now: consumer-to-consumer education. Aveda enthusiasts on forums are educating each other about products in ways that are now driving what the company builds next. "We are reacting to that education."

AI discovery favors the brands that were already building community

The panel closed on agentic commerce: the near-future scenario where an AI agent does your shopping for you. The moderator framed it as potentially threatening to community-driven brands. The three people on stage were not concerned.

Kumkum's argument: agentic discovery is fundamentally about risk reduction. Agents look for content that signals trust. If your community is talking about you authentically, those signals are exactly what an agent picks up. "If you as a brand really believe in what you stand for, your community believes in you, your community is talking about you, then agentic discovery will be more skewed towards you."

Krista added that humans still want to make the final click. Agentic AI can collapse a six-week research phase into minutes. The discovery is where agents win. The purchase remains a human moment for now. "I think people are willing to find it. The buy-it portion is still something that humans want to click."

Bill's take was the most direct: "I can't wait for it. Because of the community, because we keep firing on that engine, we're going to show up when people ask what to buy for their next fishing trip." Thirty years of trusted word-of-mouth is already optimized for an AI that weights trust signals above everything else.

One more number from Shopify's early ChatGPT referral data: men are doing the majority of shopping through ChatGPT recommendations, and the number one category they're asking about is health and wellness. The brands that built community-first in wellness are already the answer the model serves.

What to take from the room

  • Bill Neff: Yeti's European expansion started with Travis Rice's snowboard competition, with no European strategy attached. Supporting your people is the strategy. The market follows.
  • Krista Dalton: Tecovas Enthusiasts gave hour-long feedback sessions for free because someone asked. Listening without an agenda turned loyal customers into active evangelists.
  • Kumkum Pinto: consumer-to-consumer education in Aveda forums is now driving Estee Lauder product innovation. The community is telling the company what to build, not the other way around.
  • Krista built an AI chief of staff that handles weekly reporting synthesis and told her team to cancel meetings for a week and just learn Claude. The brands moving fastest on AI are experimenting now, not waiting for a policy.
  • Bill on AI-generated creative: "We want our marketing to be as handcrafted as our boots." Generative AI for creative is on hold at Yeti. AI for process, project management, and earned media listening is running everywhere.
