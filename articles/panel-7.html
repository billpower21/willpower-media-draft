Wellness House — Panel 7

Don't Have an Agenda: What Tecovas, Yeti, and Estee Lauder Know About Building Brands That Outlast the Algorithm

Krista Dalton (Tecovas CMO), Bill Neff (Yeti), and Kumkum Pinto (Estee Lauder VP Product) talked through how the brands with the strongest communities are positioned to win the AI discovery era, why treating community as a channel fails, and how real word-of-mouth is still traceable back to a single trusted person.

The moment community becomes more powerful than the marketing budget

Bill Neff has been at Yeti for years and remembers when the entire marketing strategy was word of mouth with no budget to back it. His most vivid proof point came at Memphis in May, the country's largest barbecue competition. Leanne Bakunas, who runs Yeti's culinary community, had spent years cultivating relationships with top pit masters. At Memphis in May, Bill walked through with her. "It was like we owned the place. She was walking around like the mayor. But we weren't marketing." Yeti was just the trusted product inside the barbecue community, and the community did the rest.

The second moment: Travis Rice, a snowboard ambassador, wanted to fund a competition called Natural Selection. Yeti supported it because that is what they do for ambassadors. They were not trying to break into Europe. Within weeks of the competition, shops across Europe were calling to carry Yeti. "We didn't want to go sponsor a snowboard competition to get into Europe. We wanted to support our ambassador. And then it started to ripple."

"If you domino everything back in those word-of-mouth transactions, it can get all the way back to Flip Pallot on almost every single person here."

Bill Neff, Yeti

Yeti's ambassador model: 200 people, 14 passion categories, no follower floor

Yeti works with over 200 ambassadors across fishing, hunting, barbecue, snowboarding, and more than a dozen other passion categories where a Yeti product functions as a tool in someone's specific pursuit. The philosophy: find people who can be relevant for your product within that pursuit, let the human network take over, and do not try to accelerate it with a digital platform that gets eyeballs quickly but builds nothing lasting.

"Whenever I get someone who loves Yeti, I ask how they learned about it. Generally speaking, it's from a friend." It is slower. That is the point. The network that grows at conversation speed is the one that does not evaporate when the algorithm changes.

Tecovas: Western identity walked in and never left

Krista Dalton joined Tecovas as CMO and CDO after the brand had already built 50-plus retail locations on top of a D2C-first model. What she heard from everyone she talked to when she first looked at the company: "I've been to your store, they gave me a margarita, and I loved it." Not "I love your boots." The store experience was the product that people remembered first.

Western as a category can be intimidating. How do you wear cowboy boots if you've never worn them? Tecovas stores solve that by welcoming anyone without judgment. A customer walked in before a Beyonce Cowboy Carter concert and said, "Can you just dress me?" The associate did, exactly as asked. That is community built into the retail design, not the marketing deck.

Tecovas has a Facebook group called Tecovas Enthusiasts that the brand did not start. Customers started it. When Krista's customer insights team reached out to interview members, they did not offer payment or product. They just asked for feedback. Members gave hour-long sessions and came out as evangelists. "We didn't give them anything for these interviews, but they were so thankful." Listening and responding, without an agenda, turned the most loyal customers into the loudest advocates.

"Don't have an agenda. Just be with the community. We don't worry about culture because the community shapes culture. If you're in the community, you're going to be in the culture."

Bill Neff, Yeti

Estee Lauder: protecting brand DNA across dozens of communities at scale

Kumkum Pinto manages product across Estee Lauder's portfolio of brands including MAC, Jo Malone, La Mer, Clinique, Aveda, and The Ordinary. Each brand has its own deeply distinct community and identity. The company's rule: never mess with the brand DNA. What gets shared across brands is the infrastructure behind the scenes: playbooks, data frameworks, measurement criteria, and how to win in a given category. The communities and brand identities stay completely separate.

The signal Kumkum watches for community breakdown: engagement and loyalty metrics starting to erode. Every week, Estee Lauder reviews voice-of-customer metrics and loyalty performance across D2C and retail. When numbers start slipping, the question is always the same: have we over-indexed somewhere, or have we lost trust? Most of their consumer care agents are former in-store sales associates, people who sold the products face-to-face and understand the brand from the customer's perspective, not just the corporate one.

Consumer-to-consumer education has become the most interesting force in Estee Lauder's innovation cycle over the past two to three years. Aveda enthusiasts on forums are educating each other about products in ways that are now driving what the company builds next. "We are reacting to that education."

AI discovery favors brands with real community

The panel closed on agentic commerce: the near-future scenario where an AI agent does your shopping for you. The moderator framed it as potentially threatening to community-driven brands. The brands at the table were not concerned.

Kumkum's argument: agentic discovery is fundamentally about risk reduction. Agents look for content that signals trust. If your community is talking about you authentically, those signals are exactly what an agent picks up. "If you as a brand really believe in what you stand for, your community believes in you, your community is talking about you, then agentic discovery will be more skewed towards you."

Krista added that she believes humans still want to make the final click. Agentic AI can collapse the six-week research phase for a complex purchase into minutes. The discovery is where agents win. The purchase remains a human moment for now. "I think people are willing to find it. The buy-it portion is still something that humans want to click."

Bill was enthusiastic: "I can't wait for it. Because of the community, because we keep firing on that engine, we're going to show up when people ask what to buy for their next fishing trip." The brand that spent 30 years building trusted word-of-mouth is already optimized for an AI that weights trust signals above all else.

Shopify's own early referral data from ChatGPT: men are doing the majority of shopping through ChatGPT recommendations, and the number one category they're asking about is health and wellness. Community-built wellness brands are already the answer the model serves.

Key takeaways

  • Bill Neff: Yeti's European expansion started with a single ambassador's snowboard competition, with no European strategy attached. Supporting your people is the strategy. The market follows.
  • Krista Dalton: Tecovas Enthusiasts gave hour-long feedback sessions for free because someone asked. Listening without an agenda turned loyal customers into active evangelists.
  • Kumkum Pinto: consumer-to-consumer education in Aveda forums is now driving Estee Lauder product innovation. The community is telling the company what to build, not the other way around.
  • Krista built an AI chief of staff that handles weekly reporting synthesis. She told her team to cancel as many meetings as possible one week and just learn Claude. The brands moving fastest on AI are the ones experimenting now, not waiting.
  • Bill's advice on AI-generated creative: "We want our marketing to be as handcrafted as our boots." Generative AI for creative is on hold. AI for process, project management, and earned media listening is running everywhere.
