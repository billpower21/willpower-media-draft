Greg LaVecchia Co-founder and CEO, Bloom Nutrition

The 10 years everyone skips over

People see Bloom and think two-year overnight success. Energy drink brand, Gen Z marketing, viral TikTok machine. What they miss is the 10 years before that.

Greg LaVecchia and his wife Mari started by posting her fitness transformation on Instagram in 2018. Then they ran paid ads on it. In 2018, when literally everyone else was signing coupon codes with Gymshark and waiting for the algorithm to do the work for them.

Greg's framing: treating your personal brand as a business was taboo back then. He did it anyway. That one decision, made before any of the flashy stuff, is what everything else is built on.

The four macro shifts Greg caught and none of them were on his roadmap

COVID. TikTok launching. TikTok Shop. GLP-1s.

Greg walked through each one. None of those were planned. When COVID hit, he moved to LA and built what became the largest in-house CPG influencer marketing agency in the country. When GLP-1 adoption exploded, he reformulated products to meet the Ozempic user where they were.

"You cannot predict these opportunities," he said. "I just want you to keep your eyes open for what this year's version of cheap Meta ads is. What's this year's TikTok Shop."

"I am more than happy to let Poppi buy a Super Bowl ad and tell everyone about better-for-you soda. And then we are also available in Walmart and Target."

Greg LaVecchia, Co-founder, Bloom

Let someone else pay to educate the market. Then show up cheaper and better.

Bloom was not the first greens powder. Not the first energy drink. Not the first better-for-you soda. Greg doesn't care.

Athletic Greens pays Joe Rogan to explain what a greens powder is. Red Bull sends someone to space to build the energy drink category. When they're done, Bloom's products are half the price at the same retailers, taste better, and are better formulated. "I love saturation because I hate spending money on educating people."

The Ring doorbell is his reference model. That founder didn't invent the doorbell camera concept. He made the existing idea 10x better. That's the whole play.

Move five times in five years if you have to

Philadelphia. New York. Boulder. Brooklyn. Los Angeles. Now Austin.

Each city was intentional. Boulder was a year and a half of seven-day work weeks to get away from hometown distractions. LA was the influencer hub in 2020. Austin, in Greg's words, is "the CPG entrepreneurship hub of America in 2026."

His point: most competitors won't move because moving is hard. That's the entire advantage.

100 SKUs but only two goals at a time. Always.

Someone in the room pushed back: you preach focus but Bloom has over 100 SKUs. How does that math work?

Greg's answer: most of those are flavor variants. Within the actual business, he works on two things at a time. Right now it's energy and soda. That's it. "I am 100% in Bloom for 10 years straight. I do not invest in other brands. I don't run other brands. Focus kills and that is what has let us win."

"I call them tsunamis. Very loud moments instead of peanut butter spread."

Greg LaVecchia, Co-founder, Bloom

When Bloom launched on Amazon, they bought the front page for 24 hours. When they went into Target, the goal was to turn the entire Target website green. Pick one channel, concentrate everything, take it over, then move to the next. Tsunamis, not peanut butter.

The Slumdog Millionaire principle

Bloom's top two pre-workout flavors six years ago were Glacier Crush and Crisp Apple. Today, their top two energy drink flavors are Glacier Crush and Crisp Apple. Same two. Six years apart. Data kept from a product line they no longer even sell.

Greg's point: you connect the dots looking backward. The pre-workout company was not wasted time. It was the research. "What seems like a two-year overnight success energy drink company is actually a full connecting-the-dots moment of a 10-year community-building career."

How Bloom got into Target without the traditional pitch

Old strategy to pitch Target: buy billboards near their Minneapolis HQ so buyers see you everywhere. Greg's version: find out who the Target buyers actually follow on Instagram, hire those specific creators, then email the buyers. By the time Bloom reached out, the buyers were already seeing the brand everywhere they looked. "People forget that humans work at Target. They make emotional decisions."

The Keurig Dr. Pepper deal and what it actually means

The Keurig Dr. Pepper deal happened because Bloom partnered with Nutrible first. Nutrible handles all the distribution and commercialization work. Greg focuses on what he wants to work on. Bloom products now run on Dr. Pepper trucks across America.

"One plus one equals three. We have a very large slice of a far larger pie than Bloom could have ever been if we owned the entire pie."

What Greg is watching right now

Three things: retail revenue thresholds dropping (Target used to require $30M before they'd take a meeting; he thinks that number is now closer to $10M or below), TikTok Shop still hot, and GLP-1s continuing to reshape what consumers want from functional products.

His one growth hack when asked directly: surround yourself with people younger than you. "They know more than me. I'm 30. I'm already an uncle now." The 20-year-olds in his network are already millionaires building things he can't predict. That's the point.

The only real competitive advantage over 10 years

"To win the game, you just need to stay in the game."

Greg's actual edge over the last decade: outlasting competitors who took acquisitions, went comfortable, or chased diversified portfolios. Bloom just kept showing up. "All we needed to do was outlast them." When they leave, their market share becomes yours.

What to take from the room

  • Greg scaled Bloom D2C to $25M on Shopify, then redirected his entire email and SMS list to Amazon. He sacrificed the D2C community to dominate the channel where beverages actually move. Know your lane.
  • Moonshots only. First retailer was a nationwide Target launch. "We only take large swings" is a literal strategy, not a vibe.
  • The influencer arbitrage: find who the Target buyers follow on Instagram, hire those creators, then email the buyers. Emotional buy-in from the buyer before the revenue numbers fully justified it.
  • Concentrate the marketing budget. Pick one channel, pour everything into it for a concentrated period, create the moment, then move. Peanut butter spread across six channels is six mediocre campaigns.
  • Brand power means you never start from zero. Bloom went from pre-workout to greens to energy drinks to soda without losing the community. Because the brand transferred every time.
