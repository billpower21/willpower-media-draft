10 Years, One Brand: How Greg LaVecchia Built Bloom Into a Nine-Figure Business

The co-founder of Bloom walked through every lesson that turned a fitness transformation Instagram post into 80,000 retail doors, a Keurig Dr. Pepper partnership, and the number one greens brand in U.S. retail.

Don't let pride hold you back

Greg LaVecchia and his wife Mari launched Bloom by posting her fitness transformation on Instagram and immediately treating the profile as a business. In 2018, running paid advertising to grow a personal brand felt taboo. Everyone else was signing coupon-code deals with apparel brands or waiting on the algorithm. They ran ads anyway.

"Going against the grain, feeling like or treating yourself as a business in yourself and ignoring the potential cringe of what may be taboo in society today can bring you so much further, so much faster than anyone who just has a toxic amount of pride to hold them back."

Your greatest opportunities cannot be predicted

LaVecchia identified four macro shifts that became Bloom's biggest growth windows: COVID, TikTok launching, TikTok Shop, and GLP-1s. When COVID hit, they moved to Los Angeles and built what became the largest in-house CPG influencer marketing agency. When GLP-1 adoption surged, Bloom reformulated to address that consumer's specific needs.

"Keep your eyes open and keep on the widest mindset that you can. What is this year's cheap meta ad? What is this year's COVID? What is this year's new TikTok Shop?"

Greg LaVecchia, Co-founder, Bloom

Build a platform brand. Never start over from zero.

Bloom launched as a pre-workout company. LaVecchia hasn't said the words "Bloom" and "pre-workout" in the same sentence in three years. The company now runs a nine-figure greens business and sells energy drinks and sodas. None of that required starting over because the brand and community transferred directly to every new product line.

His principle: if you build a strong enough brand, future innovations plug into it. Bloom's top two energy drink flavors, Glacier Crush and Crisp Apple, were also the top two pre-workout flavors six years earlier. The data connected across a decade.

Relocate for geographic advantage

LaVecchia and Mari moved five times in five years. Philadelphia, New York, Boulder, Brooklyn, Los Angeles, and now Austin. Each city was chosen intentionally. Boulder was a year and a half of working seven days a week to get away from hometown influences. Los Angeles was the influencer capital in 2020. Austin is, in his words, "the CPG entrepreneurship hub of America in 2026."

"Packing your bags, getting the plane ticket can be one of the largest advantages that you can have over your competition when they are probably afraid to move."

Focus kills. Two goals at a time.

LaVecchia has been 100% in Bloom for 10 straight years. No real estate portfolio, no investing in other brands. Within Bloom, he works on only two goals at a time. He calls diversification in marketing "peanut butter spread" and actively avoids it.

"I am a huge fan of putting as much as possible in a short amount of time on one channel and taking that channel over. I call them tsunamis."

Greg LaVecchia, Co-founder, Bloom

When Bloom launched on Amazon, they bought the front page for 24 hours. When they first went into Target, the goal was to turn the entire website green. "Very loud moments instead of peanut butter spread" is how he describes the strategy.

You do not need to recreate the wheel

Bloom was not the first greens powder, the first soda, or the first energy drink. LaVecchia is fine with that. Poppi buys Super Bowl ads and educates the market on better-for-you soda. Athletic Greens pays Joe Rogan to explain greens powders. Red Bull sends someone to the moon. All of them build category demand that Bloom captures at half the price in the same retail aisles.

"Education is very expensive. I am more than happy to let them go tell everyone about the category. And then we are also available in Walmart and Target."

Hire for passion, not for the org chart

LaVecchia's hiring rule: find the person who is genuinely passionate about the department they will be joining, not the person who fits a title on a competitor's org chart. The most passionate person in the role always wins long-term. He hires for his problems, which are simply the things he does not enjoy doing, so his own day stays focused on what he is best at.

The brick-and-mortar window is open now

When asked about the next major opportunity, LaVecchia pointed to retail timing. Target used to require $30M in annual sales before taking a meeting. He believes that threshold is now closer to $10M or below. "They need us more than we need them. They need to have that innovation faster than all of their competitors."

Key takeaways

  • LaVecchia scaled Bloom D2C to $25M on Shopify before touching Amazon, then redirected his entire email and SMS list to purchase on Amazon to dominate that channel.
  • Bloom's first nationwide retailer was Target, launched cold with no smaller retail history first. "Moonshots only" is tattooed on his back.
  • The arbitrage of influencer marketing was the single biggest growth unlock. Finding who Target buyers followed, then hiring those same creators, is how Bloom got shelf space before the sales numbers justified it.
  • One plus one equals three: the Nutrible partnership accelerated a deal with Keurig Dr. Pepper. Bloom products now move on Dr. Pepper trucks.
  • Bloom has had over 100 SKUs. Most are flavor variants. Focus applies to business goals, not necessarily to product line depth.
