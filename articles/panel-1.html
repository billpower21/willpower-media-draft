Cheryl built this company because one of her kids got sick and one didn't

Cheryl Sihoi had two kids born two different ways. Her daughter, C-section, ended up with eczema, allergies, and food sensitivities. Her son, vaginal birth, had nothing. Same parents. Same house. Totally different microbiomes from day one.

She went looking for the science and found it. C-section babies have a four times higher risk of obesity, asthma, and allergies because they miss the microbial transfer that happens in the birth canal. The research existed. The consumer-facing product did not. She built it.

Tiny Health now has 125,000 gut samples sequenced. That is the largest microbiome dataset for babies and toddlers in the world. When gut health finds its mainstream moment the way sleep found its moment with Eight Sleep and Whoop, Cheryl will have built the data infrastructure that nobody else has.

Scott's whole thesis is one stat: 50 million people, zero solutions

Scott Hinkle at Throne Science opened with a number that should stop every founder in the room. 50 million Americans have chronic digestive disease. One in seven people. These are not people who are intermittently uncomfortable. They are managing real daily pain and getting very little help from the medical system.

His bet: the shift from reactive to proactive health is underway, and gut health is at exactly the inflection point where sleep was five years ago. Everyone knew sleep mattered. The products that made it measurable and improvable hadn't arrived yet. Now they're everywhere.

Gut health has that same energy right now. The awareness is building. The products are coming. The brands already inside the category with the data, the community, and the trust, those are the ones who win the next five years.

"We are at a gut health revolution moment right now. This is the same place that sleep was 3-5 years ago."

Scott Hinkle, Co-founder, Throne Science

Function's physician trust problem and how they solved it

Pranitha at Function Health described the founding challenge: testing 160 biomarkers at a price that made it accessible to anyone, not just executives and professional athletes. That disrupts how labs have worked for decades.

Physicians pushed back hard. "Our results are scary" was the concern from the medical establishment. Function's answer: publish the methodology, partner with doctors rather than route around them, let the data speak over time. Results come with context and guidance, not raw numbers dropped on a patient with no support.

The flywheel is working. Function members bring their results to their doctors. The doctors see 160 biomarkers they didn't know to order. Some of those doctors become Function's most enthusiastic advocates. "Our members are bringing this to doctors who then become advocates."

Cheryl reads 10,000 customer emails personally

This one detail tells you everything about how Cheryl runs Tiny Health.

She reads customer emails herself. Not summaries. Actual emails, at volume, over years. The insight she found: customers were ordering the product as a last resort after going to multiple doctors and getting no answers. These were desperate parents with sick kids who found Tiny Health after the medical system failed them.

That changed how she talks about the product, who the advocates are, and what she builds next. You cannot get that from a dashboard. You get it by reading the emails.

"The parents that are the biggest advocates for us are the ones that got their kid healthy."

Cheryl Sihoi, Co-founder, Tiny Health

AI is changing the patient experience faster than it's changing the clinical one

All three founders agreed: AI is already reshaping how people understand and navigate their own health, and the medical system is not keeping up.

Patients are using AI to decode their test results before they talk to a doctor. They're researching diagnoses, asking follow-up questions, finding specialists their GP never mentioned. The information asymmetry that used to define the patient-physician relationship is collapsing fast.

For brands like Tiny Health and Function, that's a massive tailwind. If a potential customer asks ChatGPT how to understand their kid's gut health, the answer increasingly points toward the kind of detailed, data-backed content these companies produce. The brands that built their community on education and trust are the ones the AI serves.

The 17-year average for research to reach medical practice is not sustainable when patients can access the research today. Something is going to break. These are the founders building the products that fill the gap before it does.

What to take from the room

  • Cheryl's email practice is a repeatable strategy. Founders who stay personally connected to customer language at scale, not summaries of that language, build products that solve the right problems.
  • Function's physician playbook: when your product disrupts the medical establishment, bring doctors into the data rather than routing around them. Turn potential critics into advocates.
  • Scott's category timing framework applies beyond gut health. Find where consumer awareness is building faster than the product infrastructure. That gap is the opportunity.
  • The 17-year research-to-practice lag is a structural problem. Brands that bridge it with consumer-grade products are serving people the medical system has decided to wait on.
  • If AI surfaces your brand when patients ask questions about your category, that's earned media at a scale no ad budget can replicate. Build the content and community that AI trusts.
