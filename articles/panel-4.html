Community Is a Product, Not a Channel: What 10,000 Sweat Pals Communities and a Backyard Sauna Taught These Operators

Eric of Muscle Mountain, Salar of Sweat Pals, and Nick of Vivo Barefoot talked through what actually makes communities grow, why paid marketing alone becomes a treadmill, and why the magic number for deep connection is 6 to 10 people.

Movement as the medium

Salar moved to the United States 12 years ago and was told the way to make friends was bars and nightclubs. That environment didn't work for him. What did work: run clubs, soccer, rock climbing, weekend gym sessions. High-fiving complete strangers before knowing their names. People of different ages in the same room, all choosing to be there.

Three years ago he moved to Austin and faced the same problem: where are these experiences happening, and how do you find them? Sweat Pals was the answer. The platform now powers over 10,000 communities and more than one million active users. It handles event management, ticketing, membership, and point of sale for everything from passion-project run clubs to full-scale gym and studio operations like Squatch Fitness.

"If you don't have the community aspect, you're always in a treadmill race. You always have to do paid marketing. Community is truly how you scale it."

Salar, Co-founder, Sweat Pals

The 6-to-10 rule for deep connection

Eric of Muscle Mountain hosts contrast therapy (sauna plus cold water) at his home in Austin. Since moving in September, he has averaged five to ten people per night, six nights a week. That is roughly 1,200 people hosted in five months, not just met but actually hosted: walking through his space, grabbing products on display, doing the work together.

When asked what creates the strongest connections in those environments, Eric pointed to two things: narrowed aperture (in a hot sauna, your phone overheats and your defenses go down fast) and the curation of like-minded people who are into pushing themselves. "In a hot sauna, you get vulnerable really quick." He keeps groups at six to ten intentionally. If it gets too big, the depth of connection in 60 minutes drops off. "That 6 to 10 is the magic number for deep connection in a short amount of time."

The method: he is intentional about who he invites. He has a founders-only group for entrepreneurs, creators, and investors. The ecosystem benefits everyone because every person in the room is adding something. "The sum is greater than the parts."

Community needs a ritual

Salar referenced the book Art of Community: community is people who gather around shared identity and shared experiences. Identity alone is not enough. You need the ritual. Religion works as a community because people go to church on Sundays. That is the calling card.

What works on Sweat Pals: Squatch Fitness does the community workout on the last Saturday of every month. That consistency is what creates belonging. Show up once and you've done a workout. Show up consistently and you know people, they know you, you belong. "It's not necessarily part of a running club if you show up one time."

The types of events that build real community on the platform are not the transactional ones (come work out, get out). They are the ones with a social component after: run-and-raves, coffee-and-chill, an 80s-themed volleyball night, a Taylor Swift-themed lift club in Arizona. There is always a wellness component, but there is a curated social aspect that takes the pressure off people who just want to meet others.

Vivo Barefoot: 16 festivals, zero shoes sold

Nick joined Vivo Barefoot nine years ago when the team would celebrate just seeing someone wearing the shoes in public. The company grew 30% or more year-on-year for its first five years. But they hit a tipping point where customers were connecting with each other and self-organizing without Vivo facilitating it.

One of their biggest community bets: they built a handmade wooden trailer (involving fire and a good story Nick offered to share offline) and took it to 16 festivals across the UK. They made a deliberate decision not to sell shoes. They were there to sell the experience of what Vivo represents. Ambassadors delivered talks, workshops, and sound baths. Website traffic spiked massively by the end of the summer, but it was a build-up across the whole season, not a single spike. The ROI was hard to present to a CFO at the time. It worked because they believed it would, went all in on it, and didn't compromise.

"The first event doesn't build community. It takes time. If you do it once and try to make it too transactional, it's never going to work."

Salar, Co-founder, Sweat Pals

The difference between performance marketing and community marketing

Nick made the case for thinking about community across 360 degrees: you can't do the odd event and hope it drives your business. You need the whole picture. But the distinction matters: community is not a channel. You can run 100 events and have very little impact if you tried to reach everyone. The brands that build community pick their groups, stay consistent with those groups, and become comfortable being explicit about what they are not doing and who they are not for yet.

Vivo's Japan market focuses on climbing and hiking communities. New York is different. Each city has its own articulation of the same core values. As the company scales globally, the internal culture is what keeps the external community coherent.

What brands get wrong about community events

Eric gave the clearest answer: a fancy dinner gets you one good evening and no additional content. An ambassador summit with trail runs, rock climbing, CrossFit, and cold stream plunges gives you content from every angle, product feedback in real time, ambassadors who feel genuinely part of something, and people on the outside looking in wanting to join. 10,000 (the fitness brand) did exactly that in Denver with 15 to 20 ambassadors, two to three days, and walked away with months of content and a team that understood the products better than any briefing could achieve.

Salar's answer: trying to sell the moment someone walks in the door. "Instead of welcoming them and facilitating the connection, you're immediately trying to transact. That should always come last. The other stuff will follow naturally."

Key takeaways

  • Eric hosts 5-10 people six nights a week through contrast therapy. Compounding consistency at small scale built to 1,200 hosted in five months. "It's not being superhuman any one day. It's the compounding consistency of doing it regularly."
  • Sweat Pals sees the highest community growth in events that fuse wellness with a social element: run-and-rave, coffee chill, themed experiences. The workout is the reason they come; the social component is why they return.
  • Vivo's 16-festival summer with no shoes for sale was their biggest marketing investment at the time. Direct sale ROI was zero. Brand equity and website traffic validated it by end of season.
  • Nick's advice for brands entering a new city: partner with the local community first, rather than assuming you can pop up and draw people. "If you're Klaviyo or Shopify and you want to get to Austin, you've got Willpower."
  • Salar's framework: shared identity plus ritual equals community. Neither alone is enough. The ritual is what makes people show up consistently enough to belong.
