Wellness House — Panel 4

Eric Hosted 1,200 People in His Backyard Sauna. Salar Built 10,000 Communities on One Platform. Vivo Went to 16 Festivals Without Selling a Single Shoe. The Magic Number Across All Three Is 6 to 10.

Eric (Muscle Mountain), Salar (Sweat Pals), and Nick (Vivo Barefoot) broke down what actually makes communities compound. Spoiler: it's not the platform. It's not the event. It's the ritual that keeps people coming back.

Salar couldn't find his people when he moved to the U.S. So he built the app for that

Salar moved to the United States 12 years ago. He was told the way to make friends was bars and nightclubs. That environment didn't work for him. What did: run clubs, soccer, rock climbing, weekend gym sessions. High-fiving complete strangers before knowing their names. People of different ages and backgrounds in the same room, all choosing to be there.

Three years ago he moved to Austin and faced the same problem: where are these experiences happening, and how do you find them? Sweat Pals was the answer. The platform now powers over 10,000 communities and more than one million active users. It handles event management, ticketing, membership, and point of sale for everything from small passion-project run clubs to full-scale operations like Squatch Fitness.

"If you don't have the community aspect, you're always on a treadmill. You always have to do paid marketing. Community is truly how you scale."

Salar, Co-founder, Sweat Pals

Eric's backyard: 5-10 people, six nights a week, since September

Eric of Muscle Mountain hosts contrast therapy at his home in Austin. Sauna plus cold water. Since moving in September, he has averaged five to ten people per night, six nights a week. That is roughly 1,200 people hosted in five months.

Not met. Hosted. Walking through his space, using the products he has on hand, doing the work together.

He keeps groups at six to ten intentionally. "In a hot sauna, you get vulnerable really quick." Your phone overheats, your defenses come down, and you're just a person in a hot box with five other people who came here by choice. Go bigger than ten and the depth of connection in 60 minutes drops off fast. "That 6 to 10 is the magic number for deep connection in a short amount of time."

He also curates who he invites. Founders-only sessions for entrepreneurs, creators, and investors. The whole room adds value to itself. "The sum is greater than the parts."

Shared identity plus ritual equals community. Neither one alone is enough.

Salar referenced Art of Community: community is people who gather around shared identity and shared experiences. Identity alone doesn't do it. You need the ritual.

Religion works as a community because people go to church on Sundays. The ritual is the calling card. What works on Sweat Pals: Squatch Fitness runs a community workout on the last Saturday of every month. That single consistent touchpoint is what creates belonging. Show up once and you've done a workout. Show up consistently and you know people, they know you, you belong.

"It's not necessarily part of a running club if you show up one time."

The events that actually build real community on the platform combine a wellness component with a curated social moment afterward. Run-and-raves. Coffee-and-chill. An 80s-themed volleyball night. A Taylor Swift-themed lift club in Arizona. The workout gets them in the room. The social component makes them want to come back.

Vivo Barefoot: 16 festivals, zero shoes sold

Nick joined Vivo Barefoot nine years ago when the team would celebrate just seeing someone wearing the shoes in public. The company grew 30% or more year-on-year for its first five years. But then something interesting happened: customers started connecting with each other and self-organizing without Vivo facilitating it.

One of their biggest community bets: they built a handmade wooden trailer and took it to 16 festivals across the UK. They made a deliberate decision not to sell shoes at any of them. They were there to sell the experience of what Vivo represents. Ambassadors ran talks, workshops, and sound baths. Website traffic spiked by the end of the summer. But it was a build-up across the whole season, not a single spike, and the ROI was hard to put in a slide deck at the time.

It worked because they committed fully to it and didn't compromise. That is the whole lesson.

"The first event doesn't build community. It takes time. If you do it once and try to make it too transactional, it's never going to work."

Salar, Co-founder, Sweat Pals

What brands get wrong about community events

Eric's answer: a fancy dinner gets you one good evening and no additional content. An ambassador summit with trail runs, rock climbing, CrossFit, and cold stream plunges gives you content from every angle, product feedback in real time, and ambassadors who feel genuinely part of something. People on the outside looking in want to join. 10,000 (the fitness brand) did exactly that in Denver with 15 to 20 ambassadors over two to three days and walked away with months of content and a team that understood their products better than any briefing could achieve.

Salar's answer: trying to sell the moment someone walks in the door. "Instead of welcoming them and facilitating the connection, you're immediately trying to transact. That should always come last. The other stuff will follow naturally."

Nick's: community is not a channel. You can run 100 events and have very little impact if you tried to reach everyone at once. Pick your groups. Stay consistent with those groups. Get comfortable being explicit about what you are not doing yet.

What to take from the room

  • Eric hosts 5-10 people six nights a week and calls it compounding consistency. 1,200 people hosted in five months is the result. "It's not being superhuman any one day. It's the compounding consistency of doing it regularly."
  • Sweat Pals sees the highest community growth in events that fuse wellness with a social element. The workout gets them in. The social component makes them return.
  • Vivo's 16-festival summer with no shoes for sale was their biggest marketing investment at the time. Direct sale ROI was zero. Brand equity and website traffic validated it across the season.
  • Nick's advice for brands entering a new city: partner with the local community first, rather than assuming you can pop up and draw people. "If you're Klaviyo or Shopify and you want to get to Austin, you've got Willpower."
  • Salar's framework: shared identity plus ritual equals community. Neither alone is enough. The ritual is what makes people show up consistently enough to actually belong.
