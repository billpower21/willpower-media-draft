The Spurs don't think of themselves as a sports team

Brian is four months into his role at the Spurs and made this clear early: the organization operates like an entertainment company. San Antonio is the 25th largest city in the United States. They have never been New York or LA. That constraint forced a startup mentality for decades: scout international players before anyone else (Tony Parker, Manu Ginobili), enter secondary markets like Austin before other franchises thought it made sense, and embrace AI infrastructure before most teams had a policy on it.

"We've always had to outsmart and outthink the competition," Brian said. The most wins in the NBA since 1999 with no marquee market, no coastal media machine, and no built-in national fanbase. Outsmarting is the whole model.

"We've always had to outsmart and outthink the competition. We're not New York. We're not LA. So we were the first team to really scout international players. We were the first to consider Austin a second market."

Brian, San Antonio Spurs

Playing games there is not the same as being there

The Spurs went to Paris, played the games, and went home. They had a massive lineage in France through their international players. They showed up for the event and left. A few years later they went back to Paris without a game scheduled, just to activate: watch parties, sponsor events, presence with no agenda attached. The lesson was blunt. "Just because you play there doesn't mean you're there."

That same logic drove the Austin office. 20 Spurs employees are based in Austin full-time. They sponsored the Austin Half Marathon. They run watch parties regularly. "We look at Austin as just as much our home market as San Antonio." Other NBA teams thought they were crazy to take games away from San Antonio. The Spurs saw Austin as the same market viewed from a wider lens, the way DC, Maryland, and Virginia are really one 6.5 million person metro despite three different jurisdictions.

Evan's three-layer problem: nobody knows what Major League Pickleball is

Evan runs the Texas Ranchers franchise in Major League Pickleball. When Bill asked the room how many people play pickleball, a few hands went up. The number who know what Major League Pickleball is: smaller. The number who know the format and rules well enough to watch it and care: smaller still.

His entire community strategy is built around closing that three-layer gap. First, get people to understand the sport. Then the Texas Ranchers. Then show them how to actually be a fan and support the team. "Education has been a key investment area for us."

Last year's Austin tour stop drew almost 6,000 people. Most people in that room had no idea it existed before they showed up. The market is there. The infrastructure to be a fan just needs to be built around them. "If you give Austin fans an environment to be fans, they will be fans."

The locker room brand lesson: start with the fourth-string guy

Tyler played for the New England Patriots and later founded Stanford's NIL program. His observation from inside the locker room: the most influential players are not always the most visible ones.

The guy with 78 Instagram followers who brings his lunchbox to work every day, who models what it means to be a great teammate, who is loved by everyone in the room. That is the person the whole team is watching. Brands that only partner with the starting quarterback miss him entirely.

His proof: a brand called Upside Down Dallas ended up on Dak Prescott's head because they started with the fourth-string quarterback, who was beloved in that locker room. One relationship rippled through the entire team. "Some of the most influential people are the ones you don't know about."

"Put people in place to succeed using their strengths. You're not going to put a route runner in to block. Use that person where they're going to thrive."

Tyler, Former New England Patriots / Stanford NIL Founder

Stanford's NIL lesson: never assume the room knows what you know

Tyler's fundraising pitch for Stanford's NIL collective assumed that three years after NIL became legal, Stanford graduates knew what it was. They did not. First question at nearly every pitch: "Is this illegal? Am I laundering money?"

He had spent 50 to 100 hours pitching a concept that required him to first teach NIL 101 to every room. He had taken the macro for granted. "We took the macro of things instead of focusing on what our exact graduate needed to hear." The lesson applies to every brand building in a category that feels obvious from the inside and opaque from the outside.

Pickleball's channel strategy problem: your fans are changing faster than your model

Evan's version of the same mistake. They had a detailed picture of who the pickleball fan was. Then they went to market. In three years, the average age of the pickleball viewer dropped five to six years. It is now below 30. "They call it the Benjamin Button sport."

The lesson: reports and models are a starting point. You do not truly know who your fans are until you're in the market listening to actual behavior. The brands that stay close to their community catch the shift in real time. The ones who rely on the model from two years ago keep optimizing for an audience that has already moved.

What's coming

Tyler sees immersive sphere-style experiences shrinking to movie-theater scale, letting fans sit in the front row of any game from anywhere. Evan sees 10,000-plus fans at a Major League Pickleball event in Austin with pros in their teens (Annalee Waters, the current world number one, is 19 and just signed with Nike). Brian sees a brand new Spurs arena in downtown San Antonio, five years out, that he believes will permanently change the city and make the I-35 corridor one of the country's most watched sports markets.

The thread across all three: the communities that get built before the infrastructure is finished are the ones with the most loyalty when it arrives.

What to take from the room

  • Brian: the Spurs went to Paris without a game scheduled just to activate. Presence without a game is how you prove you're actually building there, not just touring.
  • Evan: pickleball's average fan age dropped 5-6 years in three years. A channel strategy built on last year's fan profile is already outdated. Get in the market and watch actual behavior.
  • Tyler: the fourth-string quarterback who is loved in the locker room moves product. Research which players have real influence inside the team, not just outside it. The most cost-effective ambassador relationships are in that gap.
  • The Spurs have won more NBA games than any other franchise since 1999. Most fans outside San Antonio don't know this. Winning without a marketing infrastructure to tell the story still leaves fandom on the table.
  • Tyler's NIL lesson applied to any pitch: if your category feels obvious to you and opaque to the room, you are in the education business before you are in the sales business. Build that in.
