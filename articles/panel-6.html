Wellness House — Panel 6

Beyond the Logo: How the Spurs, Major League Pickleball, and a Former Patriot Think About Building Fan Communities That Actually Last

Brian from the San Antonio Spurs, Evan from the Texas Ranchers (Major League Pickleball), and Tyler (ex-New England Patriots) talked through why showing up once is not the same as being there, how to build fandom in markets that don't know you yet, and what the locker room teaches you about brand.

Sponsorship is no longer just a logo

The panel opened with a direct challenge to the old model. A logo on a jersey or banner used to be enough to call yourself a sponsor. Today, brands and teams are both asking harder questions: how do you measure whether the sponsorship is working, and how do you drive actual sales from it?

Brian, four months into his role at the Spurs, framed the organization's philosophy clearly. The Spurs do not think of themselves as a sports team. They think of themselves as an entertainment company. San Antonio is the 25th largest city in the United States. They have never been New York or LA. That constraint forced them to operate like a startup: scout international players before anyone else (Tony Parker, Manu Ginobili), enter secondary markets like Austin before other teams thought it made sense, and now move into AI before most franchises have touched it.

"We've always had to outsmart and outthink the competition. We're not New York. We're not LA. So we were the first team to really scout international players. We were the first to consider Austin a second market. We're one of the first teams to really embrace AI."

Brian, San Antonio Spurs

Playing games there is not the same as being there

Brian shared the Spurs' most instructive mistake: playing in Paris, doing the games, then going home. They had a massive lineage in France through their international players. They showed up for the event and left. A few years later they went back to Paris without a game scheduled, just to activate: watch parties, sponsor events, planting a flag. The lesson: "Just because you play there doesn't mean you're there."

The same logic drove the Austin office. The Spurs have 20 employees based in Austin full-time. They sponsored the Austin Half Marathon. They run watch parties. "We look at Austin as just as much our home market as San Antonio." Other NBA teams thought they were crazy to take two games away from San Antonio. The Spurs saw Austin as the same market viewed from a wider lens, the way DC, Maryland, and Virginia are really one 6.5 million person metro despite three different jurisdictions.

Pickleball's education problem (and its solution)

Evan of the Texas Ranchers runs a franchise in Major League Pickleball. When Bill asked the room how many people play pickleball, a few hands went up. The number who know what Major League Pickleball is is even smaller. The number who know the rules of the MLP format is smaller still.

His entire community strategy is built around closing that three-layer gap. First, get people to understand the sport. Then get them to understand the Texas Ranchers. Then show them how to support the team. "Education has been a key investment area for us." The strategy for driving repeat fan behavior: create an environment where people can become fans, then trust that fans in a good environment will buy merchandise, go to local events, and watch on TV.

Last year's Austin tour stop drew almost 6,000 people. Most people in the room had no idea it existed. The market is there. The infrastructure to be a fan just needs to be built around them. "If you give Austin Knights an environment to be a fan, they will be fans."

The locker room brand lesson

Tyler played in the NFL (New England Patriots) and later founded Stanford's NIL program. His observation from inside the locker room: the most influential players are not always the most visible ones. The guy with 78 Instagram followers who brings his lunchbox to work every day, shows what it means to be a great teammate, and is loved by everyone in the room, that is the person the whole team is watching. When brands only partner with the starting quarterback, they miss him entirely.

His example: a brand called "Upside Down Dallas" ended up on Dak Prescott's head because they started with the fourth-string quarterback, who was beloved in the locker room. That one relationship rippled through the entire team. "Some of the most influential people are the ones you don't know about."

"Put people in place to succeed and use their strengths. You're not going to put a route runner in to block. Use that person where they're going to thrive."

Tyler, Former New England Patriots / Stanford NIL Founder

Stanford's NIL lesson: never assume the room knows what you know

Tyler's fundraising pitch for Stanford's NIL collective assumed that three years after NIL became legal, Stanford graduates knew what it was. They did not. The first question at nearly every pitch: "Is this illegal? Am I laundering money?" He had spent 50 to 100 hours pitching a concept that required him to first teach NIL 101 to every room because he had taken the macro for granted. "We took the macro of things instead of focused on what our exact graduate wanted to hear or needed to hear."

Pickleball's channel strategy problem: fans change faster than your model

Evan's version of the same mistake: they had a detailed picture of who the pickleball fan was. Then they went to market. In three years, the average age of the pickleball viewer has dropped five to six years. It is now below 30. "They call it the Benjamin Button sport." The lesson: you do not truly know who your fans are until you are out in the market listening to them and watching their behavior. Reports and models are a starting point. Field time is the data that matters.

What the next five years look like

Tyler's vision: immersive sphere-style experiences that shrink down to movie-theater scale, letting fans sit in the front row of their favorite team's game from anywhere. Evan's: 10,000-plus fans at a Major League Pickleball event in Austin, with pros in their teens and early 20s (Annalee Waters, the current world number one, is 19 and just signed with Nike). Brian's: a brand new arena in downtown San Antonio, five years out, that he believes will change the city permanently and give the I-35 corridor between Austin and San Antonio a reason to become one of the country's most watched sports markets.

Key takeaways

  • Brian: The Spurs have won more NBA games than any other team since 1999. Most fans outside San Antonio don't know this. Winning without a marketing infrastructure to tell the story still leaves fandom on the table.
  • Evan: Pickleball's average fan age has dropped 5-6 years in three years. Channel strategies built on last year's fan profile are already outdated. Get in the market and watch actual behavior.
  • Tyler: the fourth-string quarterback who is loved in the locker room moves product. The brands that research which players have real influence inside the team, not just outside it, find the most cost-effective ambassador relationships.
  • The Spurs went to Paris without a game just to activate. Presence without a game scheduled is how you prove you're actually building there, not just touring.
  • Tyler's Patriots lesson applied to business: find the skilled individuals first, then build the role around them. Don't hire for a title and hope the person fits.
