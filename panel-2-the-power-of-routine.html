The Power of Routine: How Wellness Products Become Part of Your Life

The CMOs of Whoop and Higher Dose on what separates a wellness product that transforms your daily life from one that gets used twice and forgotten — and what every brand building in the space can learn from it.

Key Takeaways
  • Behavior change is the real product. The most durable wellness brands are not selling devices or modalities — they are selling the infrastructure for a new habit. If your product cannot create a measurable behavior loop, it will not retain.
  • Start with one specific, winnable promise. Both Whoop and Higher Dose learned that trying to explain everything upfront loses everyone. Earn trust with one clear, provable benefit first — then build the stack from there.
  • Data is an input, not a report card. Products that generate anxiety will eventually get abandoned. The best wellness brands teach users to treat their data the way they treat a weather forecast: useful context, not a verdict on who you are that day.
  • Design your product around the life your customer already has. Higher Dose's entire at-home product line was built on a single insight: people will do what fits their actual routine. Spa-quality results belong in the ten minutes before your morning coffee, not on a calendar booking three weeks out.
  • Social shareability is your leading adoption signal. Before retention data matures, watch what people post. Whoop's healthspan feature became the most shared screenshot in the app within a week of launch — an early signal that the feature had earned a place in users' self-narratives.

What Makes a Wellness Product Actually Stick

Most wellness products are bought with the best of intentions. The sauna blanket gets used twice. The wearable sits in a drawer after the first month. The supplement stack gets retired when the travel bag stays packed too long. The industry has a retention problem that the industry rarely talks about honestly — and it starts with a fundamental misunderstanding of what people are actually buying when they purchase a wellness product.

They are not buying the device. They are not buying the modality. They are buying a version of themselves — one who has better sleep, more energy, clearer skin, a calmer nervous system. And the gap between that vision and daily behavior is where most wellness brands lose their customers before they ever truly earn them.

At Willpower's second panel session, John Sullivan — who has been Whoop's CMO for nearly nine years — and Ingrid Millman-Cordy, CMO of Higher Dose, sat down with Jake Hyen of Wellworthy Media to explore the mechanics of routine-building: what makes a product integrate into someone's real life versus sit unused beside it. The conversation ranged from product design philosophy to campaign failures to the surprisingly tricky question of when data becomes a burden rather than a benefit. What emerged was a framework for thinking about adoption that applies well beyond wellness.

What you can measure, you can then manage. If you can expose someone to their data with no coaching whatsoever, they improve on every measurable dimension.

John Sullivan CMO, Whoop

Behavior Stacking: The Architecture of a Lasting Habit

Sullivan frames Whoop's fundamental value proposition not as sleep tracking or strain monitoring, but as behavior change — and specifically, what he calls behavior stacking. The idea is straightforward: meaningful, lasting change doesn't come from a single grand intervention. It comes from layering small, measurable actions until they become automatic.

Whoop's early evidence for this came from an unlikely place: a study run in partnership with the Department of Defense's Explosive Ordnance Disposal unit. Half the participants were given access to their Whoop data. The other half wore the device but saw nothing. The group with data access improved on every measurable dimension of performance — with no coaching, no intervention, no program. Awareness alone changed behavior. The measurement created the feedback loop. The feedback loop created the habit.

For Higher Dose, behavior stacking takes a more tactile form. The brand built its at-home product line around a simple insight: if a wellness practice can be folded into a routine someone already has, it will stick. If it requires a separate trip, a separate booking, a separate commitment, it will eventually lose to friction. "Incorporating the routine into your life is literally the design of our products," Millman-Cordy said. Red light therapy at home, before coffee, while meditating. PEMF for ten minutes before bed — not instead of sleep, but as the transition into it.

Both brands arrived at the same design principle from different angles: the best wellness product isn't the most feature-rich one. It's the one that creates the smallest possible on-ramp and then reveals its depth over time.

The Simplicity Trap: Why Saying Everything Communicates Nothing

One of the most instructive exchanges in the panel was Sullivan's honest accounting of Whoop's early marketing mistakes. In the beginning, the team was adamant about the full picture: recovery, strain, sleep — all three, equally important, all explained together. It was accurate. It was also, he admitted, a disaster.

"I think simplicity is really, really, really important," he said. "That set us behind in the early days for how best to communicate the value proposition of the brand." What audiences needed wasn't a complete portrait of Whoop's capabilities. They needed a single clear promise they could verify quickly in their own life. Once you prove that promise, you've earned the right to tell them everything else.

Millman-Cordy describes the same philosophy at Higher Dose through what she calls the "one manageable thing" approach. Whether a customer comes in because they want better sleep, stress relief, detox support, or skin improvement — the brand meets them at that one entry point and earns trust before expanding the picture. "Let's start with that one manageable thing, market to that, earn their trust, and then come back and explain to them, actually, this is built into a ritual."

The implication for wellness brands is uncomfortable but important: your product may be genuinely multidimensional. That doesn't mean your marketing should be. The customer will discover the depth once you've gotten them through the door with a single, clear, deliverable outcome.

We start small, start very specific, and then we explain to them how the lifestyle builds from there. Our customers see their sleep score go up five points after ten minutes of PEMF — and that's the unlock.

Ingrid Millman-Cordy CMO, Higher Dose

Data Anxiety: When the Feedback Loop Becomes a Burden

The panel's moderator, Jake Hyen, raised what may be the most honest question anyone in wellness tech gets asked: what happens when the data stops helping and starts controlling? He described wearing Whoop, waking up feeling good, seeing a poor recovery score, and spending the rest of the day feeling bad because the app told him to.

It's a real phenomenon — and both panelists took it seriously rather than dismissing it. Sullivan called it a self-fulfilling prophecy and acknowledged that Whoop has built a feature specifically to address it: a blinding mode, developed in collaboration with professional sports teams, that lets users black out their recovery score in the morning. Coaches had requested it because they couldn't have athletes walking into practice with a number in their head before they'd even warmed up.

But Sullivan's deeper point was a reframe, not a workaround. "The data is an input to your lifestyle. It's not a report card for your lifestyle." A low recovery score is a weather forecast, not a verdict. It changes how you prepare, not whether you show up. That distinction — between information and judgment — is where the most thoughtful wellness brands are actively doing education work, because the brands that don't make it will eventually train their users to ignore the data entirely.

Millman-Cordy added a dimension that is notably underrepresented in the wellness tech space: the role of intuition alongside data. Higher Dose is female-founded and female-led, and the brand's perspective on tracking explicitly makes room for what the numbers cannot capture. "We also really focus on connecting with how you're actually feeling," she said. "I might wake up and my Oura Ring score might be terrible, and I might wake up and be like, actually, I feel pretty good. I'm going to go for that run." Data and self-knowledge are not in competition. The goal is to use both.

Meeting Every Kind of Customer: From Biohacker to Burnout

One of the more sophisticated pieces of Millman-Cordy's thinking is the framework she uses to segment Higher Dose's customer base — not by demographic, but by relationship to wellness itself. At one end: the optimizer. Someone who tracks everything, stacks every modality, considers their wellness practices part of their identity, and would describe Whoop and Oura Ring and continuous glucose monitors as essential tools. At the other end: the regulator. Someone managing burnout, autoimmune issues, or Lyme disease, trying simply to return to baseline.

These two customers need radically different communication. The optimizer wants data density, performance claims, and technical specificity. The regulator needs warmth, accessibility, and the reassurance that they don't need to understand the science of PEMF therapy before they're allowed to benefit from it. "It's our position as the educators, the market makers of the space, to invite those people into the brand in an empathetic and vulnerable way," Millman-Cordy said.

Whoop navigates the same spectrum by investing heavily in what Sullivan calls representation — showing people who look like the customer back to themselves. Yes, Patrick Mahomes and Cristiano Ronaldo are ambassadors. But so are parents, professionals, and people in arts and media. The throughline isn't elite performance. It's ambition, broadly defined. "Performers, ambitious people of all stripes" — the message is that if living closer to your potential matters to you, Whoop is relevant to you. That framing is both inclusive and aspirational, and it's a harder line to walk than it looks.

The idea that Whoop was only for apex performers became like shit on a shoe. As marketers, you run around trying to get things to stick — and then every once in a while it sticks so much.

John Sullivan CMO, Whoop

Sullivan's account of the "best obsessed" campaign is one of the best cautionary tales in modern wellness marketing. The campaign featured Alex Honnold and Michael Phelps, had a great soundtrack, and Sullivan was more proud of it than anything he'd made. It also nearly broke the brand's positioning. The performance-centric imagery was so compelling that it narrowed perception of Whoop's audience to elite athletes — the opposite of the expansion the company needed. You can't run out and say what you want to say. You have to meet people where what they're actually hearing. Sometimes a campaign works too well in exactly the wrong direction.

What Wellness Brands Need to Do in 2026

The panel ended with a rapid-fire round and a closing question: strip away the buzzwords and the brand deck and the category noise. What does a wellness brand actually need to do to win?

Sullivan's answer: "Prove you solve a problem very incisively, very specifically." Not a category. Not a lifestyle. One problem. One person. One proof point. The rest of the story can follow once you've earned the right to tell it.

Millman-Cordy's answer was a complement: "Add value to your consumer's life." Not to their aspiration, not to their wellness identity, not to their social feed. Their actual life, in the ten minutes before coffee, in the hour before bed, in the quiet moments between the noise of the day. That is where habits are formed and where brands either earn their place or quietly disappear.

The brands that survive the next wave of wellness — as the category matures, as competition intensifies, as consumers become more sophisticated about what actually works — will be the ones that designed for real routines, not aspirational ones. The ones that taught their customers to understand their data without becoming enslaved to it. The ones that made the on-ramp small enough to actually walk through, and the depth rich enough to keep people walking. That is the power of routine: not the habit itself, but the trust that builds when a product keeps showing up and delivering.
