Founders Rebuilding Health & the Future of Wellness

Founders from Tiny Health, Function, and Throne Science on why personalized, preventative health is no longer a niche pursuit — and how data, AI, and a broken trust in traditional medicine are reshaping everything.

Moderated by Sam O'Kee, Flex

Key Takeaways
  • Test, don't guess — data is the new foundation of personal health. Companies like Function and Tiny Health are making comprehensive biomarker and microbiome testing accessible to consumers, enabling individuals to make evidence-based decisions about their own health rather than relying on generic clinical advice.
  • The gut microbiome is having its "sleep moment." Just as sleep went from a biohacker interest to a mainstream health essential, gut health is now crossing the same threshold — with 90% of chronic conditions linked to gut imbalances, testing and intervention have never been more timely or accessible.
  • Trust is the core product in consumer health. When you're asking someone for their most intimate biological data, brand is not a marketing exercise — it is an operational standard. Founders who engage directly with early customers, respond personally to feedback, and build with clinical credibility earn a trust advantage that no ad spend can replicate.
  • AI is already transforming how people understand and act on their health data. Consumers are uploading lab results, microbiome reports, and symptom data into AI tools to get real-time interpretation — changing the role of the physician from primary authority to informed second opinion.
  • The future of healthcare is deeply personal and largely managed at home. Continuous, passive health monitoring — from toilets that detect early cancer signals to at-home microbiome tests — means most health management will shift out of clinical settings and into everyday life within a decade.

The Broken System That Launched Three Companies

Three founders walked onto a stage at Willpower's Wellness House carrying the same origin story wrapped in different packaging. Each had, at some point, turned to the conventional American healthcare system with a health problem — and found it structurally incapable of helping them. What they built in response is redefining what it means to understand your own body.

Cheryl Sihoy, founder and CEO of Tiny Health, had two children born differently — one via C-section, one via vaginal birth at home. The first had eczema, allergies, food sensitivities, and could not sleep. The second had none of those issues. When Sihoy went looking for a gut microbiome test to help course-correct her daughter's health, she found that her pediatrician, her OB-GYN, and even GI specialists had no framework for it. "It takes an average 17 years for groundbreaking research to get into medical practice," she noted. She didn't wait.

Pranitha Patil, co-founder and Chief Business Officer at Function, spent her twenties managing PCOS, prediabetes, and high cholesterol — partly genetic, partly addressable — and found herself doing manually on a spreadsheet what Function now does at scale: running 160-biomarker blood and urine panels to get a comprehensive picture of what's actually happening inside the body. "The American healthcare system wouldn't solve this problem for me," she said. So she quit graduate school and built it herself.

Scott Hinkle, co-founder of Throne Science, framed the problem differently but arrived at the same gap. His insight: there are 30 devices to track sleep, cardio, and metabolic health, but nothing that monitors GI and urinary function on a daily basis — despite the fact that the toilet is where the body leaves behind its most consistent health signals, multiple times a day. You have 50 million Americans with chronic digestive disease and another 60 million with a diagnosed urinary condition — and almost none of it is being tracked in real time.

In 2026, if you show up to your doctor's office without an informed opinion, that's unthinkable. The doctor is now by default a second opinion because you have all of this data at your fingertips already.

Scott Hinkle Co-Founder, Throne Science

Gut Health's Inflection Point

Ten years ago, Oura launched a sleep tracker to a niche audience of biohackers and performance optimizers. Today, sleep is considered foundational — if you're not fixing sleep, fixing everything else barely matters. Sihoy believes gut health is at exactly that inflection point right now. The search volume data backs her up: searches for "gut health" have surpassed searches for "sleep better" in recent years.

The science driving this shift is striking. 80% of the human immune system lives in the gut. The gut microbiome is largely formed in the first 1,000 days of life — from birth to around age three — and imbalances established during that window have been linked to 90% of chronic conditions in adult life. C-section-born babies, or babies exposed to antibiotics in early life, face four times the risk of developing asthma, allergies, and eczema compared to babies born vaginally. Most physicians are not taught any of this.

What Tiny Health has discovered through sequencing more than 125,000 samples is counterintuitive for a health culture obsessed with killing pathogens: the bigger problem in most adults is not the presence of harmful bacteria, but the absence of beneficial ones. "We're so obsessed as a society with parasites and E. coli and things like that and try to kill, kill, kill," Sihoy explained. "But it turns out that we're killing too much. What we really need to restore are beneficial functions."

Throne Science takes a parallel approach to a different part of the body. Hinkle's thesis: the toilet is the most data-rich surface in any home, visited multiple times daily by every person, yet it collects nothing. Throne is building passive, continuous monitoring that can detect hydration patterns, GI function, and — critically — microscopic blood that is the earliest detectable sign of colon, bladder, and kidney cancers. He calls it "the smoke detector for colon cancer." Colorectal cancer is now the leading cancer death in people under 50. The earliest intervention window is in the data that people are literally flushing away.

Trust as a Product, Not a Campaign

Consumer health companies occupy an unusual position in the trust economy. You are asking someone to hand over the most intimate data that exists about them — their blood biomarkers, their microbiome composition, their bodily outputs — and act on whatever you tell them about it. That is not a relationship you can manufacture with a rebrand or a celebrity partnership.

Patil framed this precisely: "You're asking people to give you their biomarker information. That is the deepest information about you. And if you sway with integrity on that front, I think you lose." Function's approach combines clinical credibility — Quest Diagnostics for lab work, trained physicians for interpretation — with a brand philosophy built from the inside out. The brand is not the logo or the color scheme. It is every decision the company makes about how it handles data, how it communicates uncertainty, and who it hires to talk to customers.

Every single member support agent, every single person talking on a panel — not just the founders — it's who you bring into your company to build the thing that I think is incredibly important.

Pranitha Patil Co-Founder & CBO, Function

Sihoy's approach to early trust-building is instructive for any founder in a science-forward category. She personally onboarded Tiny Health's first 1,000 micro-influencers on Instagram — giving them a test kit, no payment, no promotional requirement — and personally consulted each of them on their results because she wanted product feedback. For the first 10,000 customer emails that came into hello@tinyhealth.com, she read them herself. She called customers. She flagged problems before her team did. "Who is this person? Who's Cheryl calling me?" was a common reaction. But from that core of 50, 100, 1,000 deeply cared-for early customers, the word-of-mouth flywheel began to turn.

The counterpoint worth noting: as these companies scale, the founder is no longer the one making the calls. The question — one the panel didn't fully resolve — is how to systematize that founder-level intimacy into operations that can handle tens of thousands of customers without losing the quality of care that created trust in the first place.

AI, Personal Health Data, and the New Second Opinion

What makes this moment genuinely different from previous consumer health waves — the supplement boom, the quantified self movement, the wearable proliferation — is AI. Not AI as a feature, but AI as the layer that finally makes personal health data actionable at the individual level in real time.

Hinkle described what is already happening in Throne's beta users: people are screenshotting their health data — from Throne, from Function, from Tiny Health — and uploading it to ChatGPT or Claude to ask what it means, what interventions are appropriate, and what to bring to their next doctor's appointment. The clinician, in this new model, becomes a second opinion rather than the primary source of truth. "This is table stakes now," Hinkle said. The fluency WebMD promised and never delivered is finally available.

Patil shared a personal story that crystallized both the promise and the emotional stakes of this shift. When her son was found limp and unresponsive in his bed, she called 911 — and then, while waiting, uploaded his existing lab results into an AI tool. Within half an hour, she had identified what was happening. By noon, she knew everything was going to be okay. The physician arrived that evening and confirmed what she already knew. "That's just the beginning," she said. "In a year or two we're going to know exactly what to do for ourselves."

We're never going to go back. Our kids are never going to have to be in a place where they don't know more information about themselves and can't make more educated decisions. I feel so fortunate to be building in this moment.

Pranitha Patil Co-Founder & CBO, Function

Sihoy's perspective on AI carries an important caveat. Tiny Health's users regularly upload their microbiome results to general AI tools — and sometimes get confidently wrong answers. She gave a specific example: AI told a user that taking a certain probiotic would engraft the beneficial bacterium Akkermansia in their gut. But Tiny Health's longitudinal data across 125,000+ samples shows this is simply not how it works for people who lack that microbe — it does not engraft. "Don't take it with a grain of salt" is insufficient warning for a field this technical. The solution, she believes, is proprietary AI trained on longitudinal, domain-specific data — not general models applied to specialty science.

Function's inclusion in Microsoft's Copilot ecosystem and OpenAI Health's emerging category signal where the industry is heading: purpose-built, privacy-secured AI trained on verified clinical datasets, capable of giving personalized guidance that general-purpose models cannot. The companies that get there first — with both the data and the trust — will own the next chapter of consumer health.

The Next Decade: Health Managed at Home, Personalized by Default

The panel closed with a question that cut to the heart of what these founders are collectively building toward: what does health look like in ten years?

The consensus was striking in its ambition and its specificity. Hinkle predicted that most health management will shift out of the clinic and into the home — with passive monitoring so sophisticated that you will only see a specialist when you already know exactly what you need. Sihoy went further: today, 50% of children have at least one chronic condition, and 75% of adults do. "I think in ten years," she said, "we are leading companies that will bring that down to zero."

Patil's vision is the most end-state: end-of-one personalized care — a term that captures the logical conclusion of everything the panelists are building. Not a population-level treatment protocol slightly adjusted for your demographic. A genuinely individual care plan, built from your own longitudinal data, refined continuously by AI, and executed mostly outside a clinical environment. The technology for this does not fully exist yet. But three years ago, neither did accessible at-home microbiome testing. Neither did 160-biomarker blood panels delivered to your home and interpreted by AI. Neither did a toilet that could detect early cancer signals passively, daily, at home.

The timing question the panel raised — why now, and why not 2016 — has a simple answer that took a global health crisis to fully reveal. COVID fractured the public's trust in both healthcare authorities and the institutions built around them. That fracture created a movement: I need to take control of my own health. I cannot rely on a system that was not designed to optimize for me personally. The companies on this panel are not benefiting from that distrust — they are answering it.

In the meantime, the panelists offered three practical anchors for anyone navigating this transition: get sleep before anything else, because nearly every other intervention is undermined by poor sleep; understand your gut, because the data increasingly links it to everything from immunity to mental performance to cancer risk; and use AI as a tool for fluency, not a replacement for domain expertise. The future of health is personal, data-rich, and — for the first time — genuinely within reach.
