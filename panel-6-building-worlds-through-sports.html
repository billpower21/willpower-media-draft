Building Worlds Through Sports: Community, Culture & the New Playbook

From the NBA to Major League Pickleball to NIL — operators, athletes, and league builders share what it actually takes to create lasting fan loyalty in an era where showing up matters more than spending up.

Key Takeaways
  • A logo is not a community. Modern sports sponsorship has moved well beyond placing a logo on a jersey — brands and teams that drive real results are measuring engagement, tracking sales lift, and showing up where their audience actually lives.
  • Education is the first product in emerging sports. For newer leagues like Major League Pickleball, fan acquisition isn't just marketing — it's teaching people the rules, the stakes, and why they should care before you can ask them to cheer.
  • The unsung player in the locker room moves the needle. The most influential person in a team ecosystem is often the one with 78 followers — the teammate everyone respects — not the star. Brands that do their research and find those voices build deeper, more durable trust.
  • Presence beats transactions every time. Whether it's the Spurs opening an Austin office, flying to Paris without a game on the schedule, or MLP bringing a tour stop to town — actually being in a community, consistently, is what converts casual observers into loyal fans.
  • Give people an environment and they will become fans. Austin is sport-deprived and hungry. Across every panelist's experience, the single biggest lever for fan development is creating a live, immersive environment — not a digital campaign — and letting the energy spread organically from there.

Sponsorship Is Dead — Long Live Community

There was a time when sports sponsorship was a simple transaction: write a check, get your logo on the boards, call it marketing. That era is over. The panelists at Willpower's Session 6 were emphatic about this — and they came at it from different angles, representing the NBA, an emerging professional league, and the world of athlete business ventures.

"It used to be you just put your logo on something and you're a sponsor," said Brian Watkins, Chief Revenue Officer of the San Antonio Spurs. "I don't care if you're Super Coffee or you're Hyperice or you're Coca-Cola — you're in this business to drive results and drive sales. And so what we're really seeing is a big change: sponsorship is really not just putting your logo and saying you're done, but how do you measure if it's working and how do you drive sales for your company."

The shift matters because it changes the entire frame of what sports organizations and brand partners are building together. When sponsorship was a visibility play, reach was the metric. When it's a community play, the question becomes entirely different: Are you actually embedded in the way this audience lives, talks, and decides? Are fans becoming advocates? Are they bringing others in?

For Evan Morganstein, owner of the Texas Ranchers in Major League Pickleball, this isn't just a philosophy — it's an operational reality. You cannot rely on decades of inherited fandom when your sport is still building its own vocabulary. The work is slower, more deliberate, and more personal. But the compounding effect of that grassroots investment, he argued, is impossible to replicate with ad spend alone.

Until you're actually out in the market and listening to your fans and viewing their behavior, you don't truly know who your fans are.

Evan Morganstein Owner, Texas Ranchers / Major League Pickleball

Education as Fan Acquisition: The Pickleball Lesson

Walk into a Dallas Cowboys game and you can assume your audience knows the basics — the rules, the standings, what it means to root for the team. That luxury doesn't exist for newer sports properties. Major League Pickleball, still a young organization despite pickleball being a 65-year-old sport, has had to engineer fan development from the ground up.

Morganstein described the challenge in layers: first, people need to know pickleball exists as a competitive sport. Then they need to understand the rules well enough to appreciate watching. Then they need to discover the Texas Ranchers specifically — who they are, why they matter, how to follow them. "The amount of people who know the rules of Major League Pickleball is even smaller" than those who play recreationally, he noted. "Education has been a key investment area for us."

The payoff, when it works, is striking. The Texas Ranchers drew nearly 6,000 people to an Austin event — a crowd largely unfamiliar with the team beforehand. That's not discovery through advertising. It's discovery through immersion: give people an experience that meets them where they are, and conversion follows.

Morganstein also made a point that any brand operating across consumer segments should take seriously: pickleball has two very different audiences, and speaking to both requires distinct environments and messaging. There are the recreational players who enjoy a casual game with family, and there are the serious competitors and aspiring professionals — some starting at age eight, following the career of world number one Annalee Waters, who signed a Nike deal at nineteen. "If you try to speak to everybody, you're going to speak to nobody," he said. "But there are two segments that we offer different environments to participate in."

The Hidden Influencer: Why the Locker Room You Can't See Matters Most

Tyler Eifert, former NFL tight end and now an entrepreneur building consumer brands and working in the NIL space, offered one of the panel's most counterintuitive insights: the most powerful influence inside a sports team often belongs to the person no one outside the organization has heard of.

"Within a successful team, there's a culture and a community in that locker room," he said. "You get involved with the teammates that aren't necessarily in the spotlight — because there's guys on the team that every person you know about is talking about, but you don't know them. They're the heart and soul of that actual team. The guy has 78 followers. But the whole team is watching him when you don't know one thing about him."

Some of the most influential people are the ones you don't know about in the locker room. Doing your research as a brand — talking to the guys who actually move the needle — that's where the real leverage is.

Tyler Eifert Former NFL Tight End & Entrepreneur

He cited the example of a brand called Upside Down Dallas — a cap company that ended up on Dak Prescott not because they went straight to the Cowboys' star quarterback, but because they started with the fourth-string player who was beloved in the locker room. Once the hat was trusted inside the team's culture, it naturally worked its way to the most visible face. The lesson: cultural credibility flows inward before it flows outward.

This logic extends to NIL strategy at the university level. When Eifert helped found Stanford's NIL group, the team made a critical early mistake: assuming their alumni audience already understood what NIL was. Three years after collegiate athlete compensation was legalized, they launched a fundraising campaign that skipped the basics — and the first questions they fielded were about whether the program was illegal. Fifty to a hundred hours of pitching time had to be rebuilt around education that should have come first. The lesson is one every community builder encounters eventually: you cannot skip the foundation, no matter how obvious it feels from the inside.

Presence Over Transactions: How the Spurs Think About Market-Building

The San Antonio Spurs are a mid-market franchise by NBA standards — San Antonio ranks 25th in U.S. population — and yet they have won more games than any other NBA team since 1999. That record was built not on glamour but on a scrappy, startup-minded approach to finding advantages that bigger organizations overlook. Brian Watkins described this philosophy as foundational to everything the organization does, including how it approaches Austin.

"We don't think of ourselves as a sports team," Watkins said. "We think of ourselves as an entertainment company. We've always had to kind of outsmart and outthink the competition. We were the first team to really scout international players. We were one of the first to consider a secondary market. We're one of the first teams to really embrace AI in terms of moving our business forward. We've always kind of thought like a startup because we have to."

Nowhere is that philosophy more visible than in the Spurs' approach to Austin. They have maintained a full office here — with 20 employees — for four or five years. They sponsor the Austin Half Marathon. They host watch parties. They played games in Austin when other NBA teams thought they were crazy for doing it. The bet is a long one: Watkins and his team believe the Austin–San Antonio corridor is becoming one integrated market, analogous to the San Francisco–Oakland relationship, and that early, consistent community investment will define who fans identify with for decades.

The same logic applied to Paris, where the Spurs have deep roots through former international players. In the early years, the team played games there and went home — presence without permanence. They course-corrected: this year, with no game on the schedule, the team traveled to France anyway, hosting a watch party and activating with sponsors. "Just because you play there doesn't mean you're there," Watkins said. The distinction between transactional presence and genuine community membership is the entire game.

The Environment Thesis: Give Them a Reason to Show Up

One theme threaded through every panelist's experience — from pickleball to the NBA to post-NFL entrepreneurship — is what Morganstein described as the environment thesis: if you create the right conditions for fandom, fans will emerge. Austin, he argued, is an almost perfect test case for this.

Having grown up in Chicago, where he attended a two-million-person championship parade after the Blackhawks won the Stanley Cup, Morganstein arrived in Austin and was immediately struck by how sport-deprived the city felt. "Coming to Austin about eight or nine years ago, you realize just how sport-deprived I think this city really is. There's a need for more sports. There's a need for more Spurs games. There's a need for more live events." That gap, he insisted, represents opportunity.

Tyler Eifert's experience building community at Stanford reinforced this from a different angle. Stanford is not a storied football brand on the national stage — West Coast time zones mean games air when much of the country is already done for the night. The school cannot compete with Texas or Miami on star power or national following. So instead, the NIL group leaned into what was native to its environment: the Bay Area's reverence for founders and tech leaders. They brought in figures recognizable to that specific community — the CEO of Salesforce at a game, for instance — and built something that felt genuinely theirs, not borrowed from elsewhere.

"You can't be University of Texas. You can't be University of Miami. But you can lean into your own ways of how you make things cool and exciting," Eifert said. "We brought this tagline of Nerd Nation — guys were wearing glasses with the tape in between. We're trying to bring famous founders onto the field that the Bay Area would really recognize. You just try to make things fun at the end of the day. And winning, by the way, does solve a lot of problems. But if you don't make it fun and relatable to that grassroots community, you're just missing the mark."

If you give Austin Knights an environment to be a fan, they will be fans. They will rally around your team. There is a lot more room to grow here.

Evan Morganstein Owner, Texas Ranchers / Major League Pickleball

What all three panelists ultimately converged on is a simple but difficult truth: community cannot be bought in a media plan and it cannot be manufactured in a campaign. It is built through the accumulation of genuine presence — the office you keep open, the event you fly to without a business case, the athlete whose locker room credibility you invest in before they have a following, the arena experience that makes a first-time attendee feel like they belong. The organizations that do this work — slowly, patiently, and without expectation of immediate return — are the ones who find themselves owning a city, a sport, or a culture a decade later. And then it compounds.
