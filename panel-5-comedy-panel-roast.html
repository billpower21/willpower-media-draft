Willpower Panel Series — Session 5

The Wellness Roast: Truth Told Sideways

Five CPG and wellness founders walked into a comedy roast. What emerged — between the burns, the kava cans, and the decibel reader — was a surprisingly sharp autopsy of where the wellness industry is genuinely winning, where it's running on vibes, and why the most honest origin stories tend to start with someone getting embarrassed.

Willpower Wellness House, 2025
Hosted by Noah (Pitch Roast Live) & Ryan Springer, Midnight Venture Partners

Key Takeaways
  • Profitability is the new punk. In a room full of wellness founders, the most surprising flex wasn't a fancy product — it was Super Coffee posting 12% EBITDA margins after burning $40M at its peak. Turning a high-cash-burn brand into a profitable one is the unglamorous work nobody talks about enough.
  • The best products solve a problem the founder personally had. Every founder on stage built something because they hit a wall: Jake couldn't find a healthy coffee, Matt couldn't put his phone down, Connor got told there was no Wi-Fi at a cold plunge. The most credible pitches in wellness are built from genuine frustration, not market research.
  • Behavior change is harder to sell than a supplement. Lofty's "biggest competitor is people with self-control" is a funny line — and also a real strategic challenge. Products that ask users to change habits face a fundamentally different retention curve than products that slot into existing ones.
  • Category education is table stakes in functional beverages. Kaviva's challenge — distinguishing kava from kratom in consumer minds — is a preview of every emerging-ingredient brand's education burden. You're not just marketing your product; you're marketing the category, the plant, and the distinction between safe and sketchy.
  • Authentic communities beat manufactured hype. Connor from Jevity won the decibel-reader competition by a landslide — not through a polished pitch but because his members were genuinely in the room cheering. The crowd noise you can't fake is the signal investors should be paying attention to.

Why a Roast Is Actually the Most Honest Format in Wellness

There is a certain kind of wellness panel where everyone agrees that community is important, that authenticity matters, and that the future is personalized. These panels are fine. They produce good quotes and decent LinkedIn posts. They are also, fairly often, a little bit dishonest — curated versions of business reality delivered by people who have been coached on messaging.

The Willpower Comedy Roast is not that panel. Hosted by comedian and Pitch Roast Live founder Noah, with roasters Mike Eaton, Sam Minney, and Tonight Show veteran Pete Lee — and a venture investor, Ryan Springer of Midnight Venture Partners, sitting in an ostentatiously taller chair — this format does something unique: it strips away the prepared messaging and forces founders to respond in real time to questions they definitely did not rehearse.

What comes out is surprisingly valuable. When Jake DeCicco of Super Coffee is asked point-blank when his older brother Jimmy left the company — and when things started getting better — his unscripted answer ("We fired him two years ago. The day after Jimmy left, we got profitable") is more revealing about the dynamics of family-founded CPG companies than any case study. Comedy creates permission to say true things that decorum usually suppresses.

This is what the best roasts do. The jokes are the delivery mechanism. The payload is honesty.

Your product's biggest competitor is just people with self-control. I love that.

Mike Eaton Comedian, Cutting Fat Podcast — to Matt Hassett of Lofty

The "Strategic Reset" and Other Things Nobody Says Out Loud

Super Coffee's Jake DeCicco gave the most structurally interesting pitch of the session — not because the current numbers are impressive (though they are), but because he included the part most founders cut from their deck. There is a slide, tucked between the awards and the growth trajectory, labeled "Strategic Reset." It represents a period when Super Coffee, then ranked America's fastest-growing food and beverage business, was burning $40 million a year.

"We've raised $200 million all time," DeCicco said matter-of-factly. Then, gesturing to the right side of the slide: "And then you see growth and profitability." The pivot from high-cash-burn to a business running 12% EBITDA margins within the notoriously thin-margin beverage category is a genuine operational achievement — and it's the kind of story that rarely gets told because the industry's reward systems favor fast growth narratives over hard-won efficiency stories.

The comedians, to their credit, actually surfaced the business insight here. Ryan Springer's question — when did Jimmy leave, and when did things start getting better — wasn't a throwaway joke. It was, in a roast format, the sharpest due-diligence question in the room. The honest answer DeCicco gave was more useful to any CPG founder watching than a hundred sanitized founder interviews: sometimes the reset is the story. Sometimes the strategy is letting go of something — or someone — that isn't working.

DeCicco also made an underappreciated macro point: one in eight Americans are currently on GLP-1 medications, and one in four households has at least one GLP-1 user. When a household adopts GLP-1, the shopping habits of the entire household change. For a better-for-you coffee brand built around low sugar and high protein, that is a powerful secular tailwind — one that has very little to do with brand marketing and everything to do with demographic timing. The companies that positioned early for this shift are going to look very smart in retrospect.

The Friction Problem: Selling Behavior Change in a Low-Willpower World

Matt Hassett founded Lofty in 2018 after recognizing that his phone addiction had a specific worst-case location: the bedroom. The insight was clean. The solution was elegant. And the roasters identified the central strategic challenge of his entire business in one sentence: your biggest competitor is people with self-control.

That is, improbably, both a great joke and a genuinely hard business problem. Lofty makes a premium alarm clock designed to replace your phone on your nightstand, and a physical NFC card that blocks your most distracting apps with a single tap. These are well-designed products solving a real problem. They are also products that require the user to want to change — and to want it consistently enough to actually use the card, buy the clock, and leave the phone outside.

Hassett acknowledged this openly: most interventions people try — grayscale screens, deleting and reinstalling Instagram, screen time widgets — aren't enough. What Lofty bets on is friction. Not blocking, not surveillance, not shame — just enough physical inconvenience that you open Instagram slightly less often. It's a minimal-viable-willpower play: not asking users to be better people, just asking them to put one more obstacle between themselves and the scroll.

The early data suggests it's working. Lofty has shipped over 200,000 alarm clocks worldwide and held Wirecutter's pick for best alarm clock for six consecutive years. But the more interesting number is behavioral: users who adopt the Lofty card report more presence, more focus, and a subjective sense of feeling healthier. The product that wins the behavior change market isn't the one that demands the most from users — it's the one that lowers the activation energy just enough.

Why are work and wellness separate? That's the question. I hopped in a cold plunge, and they told me there was no Wi-Fi allowed. That was the ice bath epiphany.

Connor McAleck Founder & CEO, Jevity Club

Building the Category You Want to Win: Lessons from Kava, Cold Plunges, and Sales Tax

Three of the five founders in this session are doing something harder than selling a product — they are building the conceptual category their product lives in. This is a different kind of work, and the roast format surfaced how each of them is navigating it.

Spencer McMain's Kaviva kava seltzer faces the most acute version of this challenge. Kava is a legitimate, well-studied plant from the South Pacific with a genuine pharmacological effect: it calms the central nervous system, creates mild social energy, and produces no hangover and no inebriation. It is, on paper, an ideal alcohol alternative for a moment when 40% of Americans identify as "sober curious" and 54% report actively reducing alcohol consumption.

But kava shares a category shelf — and, in consumer minds, near-identical branding — with kratom, a different plant that is addictive, poorly regulated, and genuinely problematic. The two products are frequently displayed side by side. Consumers who have read about kratom's risks often dismiss kava by association. McMain's go-to-market challenge is not just distribution or taste. It is education at scale: teaching a mass consumer audience that two plants starting with K are actually as different as tequila and NyQuil.

Connor McAleck's Jevity Club faces a softer version of the same problem. His concept — a private wellness workspace and social club where you can cold plunge between Zoom calls — requires customers to understand and value a product category that didn't exist until very recently. The comedians tried to reduce it to "a standing desk in a sauna," which is funny and also not entirely wrong. What McAleck is actually selling is the permission to integrate recovery into the workday — a mindset shift that still requires explaining to most people in professional environments. The fact that Jevity already had 250 members within weeks of opening, with 60% of them founders and entrepreneurs, suggests the early adopter segment is real. The question is always: how large is the mainstream?

Sam Harper's Kintsugi occupies the most unsexy corner of the founder experience — sales tax compliance — and spent its panel time being roasted for being boring. Which is accurate. It is boring. It is also, as Harper noted, the kind of problem that lands people in handcuffs if they ignore it. The gap between "this is fascinating" and "this is important" is where B2B software companies live, and Kintsugi is making a credible run at $150M in valuation with 6,000 active customers by being the company that handles the thing no founder wants to think about. Sometimes the unglamorous category is the defensible one.

What the Crowd Noise Tells You

The Willpower Roast ends with a decibel reader. Each founder gets thirty seconds of crowd applause, measured objectively. It is the most democratic possible verdict: no slides, no polished metrics, no follow-up questions — just how loud the room gets when your name is called.

Lofty scored 96.6 decibels. Kintsugi hit 101.3 on a second attempt. Then Connor McAleck of Jevity took his turn, and the room erupted with the kind of sustained noise that made measuring it somewhat redundant. A clear winner.

What does it mean that a wellness workspace and social club won a roomful of wellness founders and operators? It is worth thinking about for a moment. Jevity is not the most scalable business in the room. It is not the one with the highest margins or the biggest TAM. It is the one where, when the founder walks on stage, his actual members are in the audience cheering. The product has created real community — not a community as a marketing strategy, but actual people who feel something about the place they go to work and recover.

This is the thing that is very difficult to fake and very difficult to build deliberately. It either exists or it doesn't. And when it exists, the crowd noise is unmistakable.

You won't text your ex unless you want to. That's the Kaviva guarantee.

Spencer McMain Founder, Kaviva

The broader lesson from this session is one that the roast format actually enforces rather than undermines: the founders who hold up best under unscripted pressure are the ones who know their business cold. Jake DeCicco can rattle off his repurchase rate, his latest four-week volume growth, his EBITDA margin, and his GLP-1 tailwind thesis without a single slide. Matt Hassett can articulate in one sentence why the alarm clock market needed him. Connor McAleck can describe his square footage, his membership tiers, and his expansion plans off the top of his head. That kind of operational fluency doesn't happen by accident. It happens when a founder has thought about nothing else for years.

Comedy panels surface this quality in a way that polished panel formats often don't. When the roasters are asking whether your alarm clock comes with a fleshlight attachment, you find out pretty quickly who knows their product and who knows their pitch deck. They are not always the same thing. At this event, they mostly were — which is the highest possible compliment, disguised as a burn.
