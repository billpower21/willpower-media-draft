The Future of Commerce: Brand, Community & AI

Leaders from Tecovas, YETI, and Estée Lauder Companies discuss why authentic community is the only competitive moat that compounds — and why it's the brand asset most likely to survive the rise of agentic commerce.

Key Takeaways
  • Community is the only moat that compounds. YETI grew from a fishing brand into 13+ passion verticals through ambassador word-of-mouth, not advertising spend. The compounding effect is nearly impossible to reverse-engineer.
  • Voice of customer is your most underused growth lever. Tecovas's unpaid superfan interviews — zero incentive offered — produced more loyalty-building signal than any campaign. Listening, it turns out, is its own reward program.
  • AI should amplify human connection, not replace it. The brands winning with AI are deploying it upstream: synthesizing reports, surfacing VOC signals, and accelerating internal workflows — not swapping out the human moments that build trust.
  • Agentic commerce rewards community-first brands. When AI agents recommend products, they'll surface brands with the most trusted content and the most active community signals. The brands building community today are training tomorrow's algorithms.
  • Don't treat community as a channel — treat it as a product. Invest in it. Evolve it. Meet people where they are. The brands that win aren't extracting value from communities; they're genuinely part of them.

From Boots to Culture: How Commerce Becomes Community

Tecovas opened its first retail store with a clear product thesis: sell handcrafted cowboy boots to people who appreciate quality. What they discovered was something else entirely. People didn't leave the store talking about the boots. They left talking about the experience — the associates, the atmosphere, the margarita someone handed them at the door.

"When I was first looking at Tecovas," said Krista Dalton, the brand's CMO and CDO, "the thing everyone told me was, 'I've been to your store, they gave me a margarita, and I loved it.' And everybody I met didn't say 'I love your boots.' They more so talked about the store experience."

That moment of recognition — that you've built something bigger than a product — is the inflection point every brand wants to find and almost none can plan for. The Western market, Dalton noted, carries a particular energy: it's genuinely welcoming. A customer walked into one of their stores ahead of Beyoncé's Cowboy Carter tour asking to be dressed from scratch. The associate's response wasn't a sales pitch. It was an invitation. "Let's find things that make you feel comfortable and confident." That's not a transaction. That's community.

Don't treat community as a channel. Treat it as a product. Actually invest in it. Help grow it. Evolve it as you do your product — not as a campaign.

Bill Neff VP of Marketing, YETI

The Human Network: Why YETI Doesn't Need a Million Followers

YETI is a brand people have tattooed on their bodies. It generates the kind of loyalty most marketing teams spend entire careers chasing — and it was built almost entirely through a philosophy that runs counter to the modern attention economy: invest in humans, not platforms.

Bill Neff, YETI's VP of Marketing, described an ambassador program of 200+ people across more than 13 passion verticals — fishing, barbecue, hunting, snowboarding, culinary arts, and more. Most of them have small followings by influencer standards. That's the point.

"We believe in the human network," Neff said. "We believe that our product serves as a tool for a specific passion or pursuit. And we let the human network take over as opposed to a digital platform that can get a lot of eyeballs on things really fast. It's slower, but that's the philosophy we work in."

The proof-of-concept moment came at Memphis in May, the massive barbecue competition where YETI had no advertising presence — just Leanne Bakunas, their culinary community lead, and years of showing up for pit masters who trusted the product. "It was like we owned the place," Neff recalled. "She was walking around like the mayor, but we weren't marketing."

A snowboarding documentary YETI funded for ambassador Travis Rice — not a Europe strategy, simply support for something meaningful to their community — resulted in European distributors calling off the hook asking how to carry YETI. The brand hadn't planned to enter Europe yet. Community moved faster than strategy.

Voice of Customer at Scale: The Superfan Advantage

Somewhere on Facebook, there exists a group called Tecovas Enthusiasts. YETI didn't start it — the customers did. They have opinions about materials, quality assurance, and production decisions that most brand teams would pay consultants to surface.

Dalton's team reached out asking if members would give feedback. The response was immediate and enthusiastic. Hour-long sessions, no compensation offered, no promotional ask attached. "We didn't meet with them to be like, 'this is why we do it, go tell people,'" she said. "We met with them to get feedback, to adjust, to meet the needs of our most loyal customers. And they 10x'd the word of mouth — and we didn't give them anything."

The consumer-to-consumer education I see across forums — with some of our iconic brands, specifically Aveda — is driving innovation within the company. We are reacting to that education.

Kumkum Pinto VP of Product, Estée Lauder Companies

For Estée Lauder, the signal doesn't come from a Facebook group but from the systems Kumkum Pinto's team has built to surface it continuously. VOC metrics, loyalty signals, engagement data across D2C and retail — reviewed weekly, brand by brand. "The whole idea is you have to start seeing signs of engagement decline," she said. "As it starts declining, you know something's wrong because you've either over-indexed in some areas or you're losing trust."

Most brands look at this data by channel. Estée Lauder has deliberately moved to a brand-first view — asking not how the DTC channel performed, but what people are actually saying about the brand, regardless of where the interaction happened. The nuance matters because communities don't live inside channels. They spill across them.

AI in Commerce: What the Best Operators Are Actually Doing

The conversation on AI was notable for what the panelists were not doing: none of them were using it to replace the customer relationship. All of them were using it to protect and extend it.

The AI Chief of Staff

Dalton described building an AI agent that synthesized 16 reports a week into a single executive brief — and then, three months later, replacing that with a more sophisticated AI chief of staff connected directly to Tecovas's enterprise data warehouse. Her directive to her team the previous week: cancel as many meetings as possible and spend the time exploring what AI could accelerate. "We're a private company, so our risk tolerance is higher," she noted. "The team was able to play and integrate into getting all the data we need."

The Skin Consultation Bot

At The Ordinary (an Estée Lauder brand), Pinto's team has deployed an AI agent that helps customers navigate complex ingredient combinations and skin routines — a genuinely hard knowledge problem that store associates used to handle. The goal isn't containment, she emphasized. It's giving customers quality information they couldn't previously access consistently, and then seamlessly transitioning them to a physical experience when they're ready to go deeper. "At what point are you putting enough guardrails to direct someone to speak to a human? I'm not saying we've got it right, but that's the aspiration."

The VOC Aggregator

Neff recommended a tool called Unwrap — which scrapes social media, converts customer service calls to text, and aggregates reviews into a weekly digest flagging anomalies. One week it surfaced a spike of customers reporting they weren't offered a drink at Tecovas stores. Dalton's head of retail saw it and immediately called the stores. "It's voice of the customer at scale," Neff said. "And they've been incredible for us."

AI enhances skilled people. When you blindly trust AI, that's where I get worried it goes off the rails. Our people are experts in their areas — the question is how AI helps them, not replaces them.

Bill Neff VP of Marketing, YETI

Neff's instinct on generative AI is worth noting for consumer brands in particular: YETI has deliberately set it aside for marketing content — for now. "We want our marketing to be as handcrafted as our boots," he said. The creative standard that built the brand is non-negotiable. Process automation? Absolutely. Creative output? Not yet.

Agentic Commerce: The Community-First Brands Will Win

The panel's most forward-looking exchange centered on what happens when AI agents start making purchase recommendations — and whether the community strategies these brands have built will survive the transition.

The moderator framed the concern directly: an agentic shopping assistant won't care about brand story. It'll optimize for the user's preferences and past behavior. Where does that leave brands that have spent years building emotional connection?

The panelists pushed back — and their reasoning converged on the same point.

Pinto argued that agentic discovery precedes agentic commerce — and discovery is fundamentally about risk reduction. "Agents are looking for content which brings trust, where people are trusting, where people are speaking about stuff. If your community believes in you, and they're talking about you — whether long-form or short-form — then agent discovery will be more skewed towards you."

Dalton made a distinction the data is starting to validate: she believes consumers will embrace AI for the research and discovery phase — narrowing a six-week consideration journey into an afternoon — but will still want to be the ones who click buy. "The buy-it portion is still something humans want to click. They like that moment."

Neff was the most bullish: "I can't wait for agentic commerce, quite frankly. When people are like, 'what should I buy for my next fishing trip?' — we're going to show up. We're going to show up because of the community, because we keep firing on that engine and the trust of our products. I can't wait to get out of the whole ad-search-Google game."

The early data from Shopify's internal tracking bears this out. ChatGPT referral purchases skew heavily toward categories — health, beauty, home — where the purchase requires trust and some level of education. These are precisely the categories where strong brand community creates the content and authority that AI systems index on.

The implication: brands investing in authentic community today are, without necessarily intending to, building the training data and trust signals that will determine which brands surface when the agent starts answering questions.

What to Change About How Brands Think About Community

The panel closed with a single question: if you could change one thing about how brands approach community, what would it be?

Neff's answer: "Don't have an agenda. Just be with the community. We don't worry about culture because the community shapes culture. If you're in the community, you're going to be in the culture of the community. We're not trying to get something out of the community. We're just a part of the community."

Pinto: "Don't like them as they are — don't try to shape them. Meet them where they're at."

Dalton's final word was the most counterintuitive: don't treat community as a marketing channel. "Treat it as a product. Actually invest in the community. Help grow the community. Evolve it as you do your product."

In a landscape where every brand is chasing the same paid audiences, the same influencer relationships, and the same algorithmic shortcuts — these three brands have built something that can't be bought on a media plan. They built communities that exist independent of any platform, that grow through human trust, and that are, as it turns out, exactly the kind of brand signal that the next generation of AI commerce systems will be designed to find.

If you have a community, if you have that powerful brand, even in a world where AI agents control your narrative a little bit — your narrative will still stick out. Because you've worked on community.

Glenn Begwein Office of the President, Shopify
